Options Pricing

1.1 No-Arbitrage Principle

An arbitrage is a trading strategy that: (1) costs nothing to set up, (2) has zero probability of loss, and (3) has positive probability of profit. Equivalently: a risk-free profit with no initial capital.

Law of One Price
Two portfolios with identical future payoffs in every possible state of the world must have the same price today. If they don't, an arbitrage exists.

Why it anchors all derivatives pricing: the law of one price lets us replicate derivative payoffs using traded assets (stocks + bonds), then price the derivative by the cost of the replicating portfolio — no assumptions about investor preferences or risk aversion needed.

Key No-Arbitrage Bounds (European Options)

BoundCall $C$Put $P$Reasoning
Lower bound$C \geq \max(S - Ke^{-rT},\,0)$$P \geq \max(Ke^{-rT} - S,\,0)$Can always exercise or sell; early exercise value
Upper bound$C \leq S$$P \leq Ke^{-rT}$Call never worth more than stock; put bounded by PV of strike
Non-negativity$C \geq 0$$P \geq 0$Limited liability
⚠ American vs European
For a European call on a non-dividend-paying stock, early exercise is never optimal — it is always better to sell the call than exercise it (you give up the time value). Therefore $C_\text{American} = C_\text{European}$. This is NOT true for American puts or for options on dividend-paying stocks.

1.2 Risk-Neutral Pricing

The key insight: in a complete, arbitrage-free market, there exists a unique risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$ under which all asset prices, when discounted at the risk-free rate, are martingales.

Fundamental Theorem of Asset Pricing
A market is arbitrage-free iff there exists an equivalent martingale measure $\mathbb{Q}$.
A market is complete iff this measure is unique.

Under $\mathbb{Q}$: $\quad e^{-rt}S_t$ is a martingale $\Rightarrow$ $E^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = S_t e^{r(T-t)}$

This gives the risk-neutral pricing formula for any derivative with payoff $\Phi(S_T)$:

$$V_t = e^{-r(T-t)}\,E^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[\Phi(S_T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t\right]$$

Intuition: under $\mathbb{Q}$, all assets grow at the risk-free rate (investors are "risk-neutral" — they don't require a premium for risk). Real-world probabilities and investor preferences drop out completely. The real-world drift $\mu$ is replaced by $r$ via Girsanov's theorem (Section 5).

💡 Risk-Neutral ≠ Real-World Probabilities
The risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$ is a mathematical construction — not what investors actually believe. Under $\mathbb{Q}$, a stock's expected return is $r$ (the risk-free rate). In reality (under $\mathbb{P}$), the expected return is $\mu > r$ to compensate for risk. The difference $\theta = (\mu-r)/\sigma$ is the market price of risk.

1.3 Black-Scholes Formula

Assume $S_t$ follows GBM: $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$ under $\mathbb{P}$, or $dS = rS\,dt + \sigma S\,dW^{\mathbb{Q}}$ under $\mathbb{Q}$. The closed-form price of a European call is:

Black-Scholes Call Formula
$$C = S\,\Phi(d_1) - K e^{-rT}\,\Phi(d_2)$$ $$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$$
Black-Scholes Put Formula
$$P = K e^{-rT}\,\Phi(-d_2) - S\,\Phi(-d_1)$$

Notation

  • $S$ = current stock price, $K$ = strike, $T$ = time to expiry (years)
  • $r$ = continuously compounded risk-free rate, $\sigma$ = annualised volatility
  • $\Phi(\cdot)$ = standard normal CDF, $\phi(\cdot)$ = standard normal PDF

Interpretation of Terms

TermEconomic interpretation
$S\,\Phi(d_1)$PV of receiving the stock conditional on exercise (risk-neutral probability of exercise × forward stock price)
$Ke^{-rT}\Phi(d_2)$PV of paying the strike — $\Phi(d_2)$ is the risk-neutral probability of the option expiring in-the-money
$\Phi(d_2)$Risk-neutral probability that $S_T > K$ at expiry
$\Phi(d_1)$Delta of the call (also related to the stock's contribution, not a pure probability)
📋 Worked Example (Verified)
$S=100$, $K=100$ (ATM), $T=1\text{yr}$, $r=5\%$, $\sigma=20\%$:

$d_1 = \frac{\ln(1) + (0.05+0.02)\times1}{0.20} = \frac{0.07}{0.20} = 0.35$
$d_2 = 0.35 - 0.20 = 0.15$
$\Phi(0.35) = 0.6368$, $\Phi(0.15) = 0.5596$

$C = 100 \times 0.6368 - 100 \times e^{-0.05} \times 0.5596 = 63.68 - 53.22 = \mathbf{10.45}$
$P = 100 \times e^{-0.05} \times (1-0.5596) - 100\times(1-0.6368) = \mathbf{5.57}$ ✓ (put-call parity: $C-P=4.88=S-Ke^{-rT}$)

BS Model Assumptions

  • Log-returns are normally distributed (constant $\sigma$)
  • No dividends, no transaction costs, continuous trading
  • Constant risk-free rate $r$
  • Arbitrage-free market; short selling allowed
⚠ BS Limitations — What Interviewers Probe
  • Volatility smile: Implied vol varies with strike — BS assumes constant $\sigma$. OTM puts trade at higher implied vol (skew) due to crash risk and demand for protection.
  • Fat tails: Real return distributions have excess kurtosis — BS underprices tail options.
  • No jumps: BS paths are continuous; crashes (discrete jumps) are not modelled.
  • Constant rates: For long-dated options, stochastic interest rates matter.

1.4 Black-Scholes PDE

The BS formula is the unique solution to the Black-Scholes PDE with the appropriate boundary condition. The PDE is derived via delta hedging (Section 4, §4.4 of the Calculus notes):

Black-Scholes PDE
$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - rV = 0$$ $$\text{Terminal condition: } V(S,T) = \text{Payoff}(S)$$

In Greeks notation (call options): $\quad \Theta + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\Gamma + rS\Delta - rC = 0$

This identity holds at every point on the option surface and is a testable interview question: given any three of $\{\Theta, \Gamma, \Delta, C\}$, compute the fourth.

📋 BS PDE Numerical Check (ATM call above)
$\Delta=0.6368$, $\Gamma=0.01876$, $\Theta=-6.414$/yr, $C=10.45$:

$\Theta + \frac{1}{2}(0.04)(10000)(0.01876) + (0.05)(100)(0.6368) - (0.05)(10.45)$
$= -6.414 + 3.752 + 3.184 - 0.523 = \mathbf{-0.001} \approx 0$ ✓

1.5 Put-Call Parity

For European options on a non-dividend-paying stock, one of the most important relationships in all of derivatives:

Put-Call Parity
$$C - P = S - K e^{-rT}$$ equivalently: $C + Ke^{-rT} = P + S$
Proof (no-arbitrage): Consider two portfolios at time 0:
  • Portfolio A: long call + invest $Ke^{-rT}$ at risk-free rate
  • Portfolio B: long put + long stock
At expiry $T$: both pay $\max(S_T, K)$ in all states. Therefore they cost the same today: $C + Ke^{-rT} = P + S$.

Applications of Put-Call Parity

ApplicationFormula
Implied put from call price$P = C - S + Ke^{-rT}$
Synthetic forward (long call, short put)$C - P = F\cdot e^{-rT}$ where $F=Se^{rT}$
Implied forward rate$F = (C-P)e^{rT} + K$ — detects mispricing
Implied dividendsDividend-adjusted: $C - P = S e^{-qT} - Ke^{-rT}$ where $q$ is continuous dividend yield
📈 Practical Use in Trading
Put-call parity is used to check for mispricings: if $C - P \neq S - Ke^{-rT}$, an arbitrage exists. In practice, deviations occur due to bid-ask spreads, borrowing costs (for short selling), and hard-to-borrow stocks. The "implied forward" from call and put prices is often more accurate than the listed forward price.

1.6 Binomial Tree Model

The binomial tree discretises time and stock price movement into up/down steps, providing a simple, intuitive path to option pricing without stochastic calculus.

One-Step Model

Over a single period of length $T$: stock goes to $uS$ (up) with probability $p_u$ or $dS$ (down) with probability $p_d = 1-p_u$. No-arbitrage requires $d < e^{rT} < u$.

Risk-Neutral Probability
$$p = \frac{e^{r\Delta t} - d}{u - d}$$ $$V_0 = e^{-r\Delta t}\left[p\,V_u + (1-p)\,V_d\right]$$

Note: the risk-neutral probability $p$ is uniquely determined by no-arbitrage — no estimate of the true probability $p_u$ is needed.

Cox-Ross-Rubinstein (CRR) Parameterisation

For $N$ steps over time $T$ with $\Delta t = T/N$:

$$u = e^{\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}}, \qquad d = e^{-\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}} = \frac{1}{u}$$ $$p = \frac{e^{r\Delta t} - d}{u - d}$$

As $N \to \infty$: the CRR binomial tree converges to the Black-Scholes formula.

ATM call: $S=K=100$, $T=1$yr, $r=5\%$, $\sigma=20\%$. BS exact price: 10.4506.

Convergence is $O(1/N)$ — alternating above/below the BS price (even/odd step oscillation).

Backward Induction Algorithm

  1. Build stock price lattice: $S_{i,j} = S_0\,u^{N-j}\,d^{j}$ at node $(i,j)$ (step $i$, $j$ down-moves)
  2. Compute terminal payoffs at $t=T$: $V_{N,j} = \max(S_{N,j}-K, 0)$ for a call
  3. Work backwards: $V_{i,j} = e^{-r\Delta t}\left[p\,V_{i+1,j} + (1-p)\,V_{i+1,j+1}\right]$
  4. For American options: at each node, also check early-exercise value $\max(V_{i,j}, \text{intrinsic})$
  5. $V_{0,0}$ is the option price
💡 Why the Binomial Tree Matters Beyond BS
The binomial tree is the standard method for pricing American options (where early exercise can be optimal — no closed-form BS equivalent exists). It also naturally handles path-dependent features and discrete dividends, and is widely used in practice for equity derivatives.

1.7 Monte Carlo Option Pricing

Monte Carlo exploits the risk-neutral pricing formula directly: simulate many paths of $S_T$ under $\mathbb{Q}$, compute the payoff for each path, and average.

Monte Carlo Pricing Algorithm
  1. Simulate $N$ paths: $S_T^{(i)} = S_0\exp\!\left((r-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T + \sigma\sqrt{T}\,Z^{(i)}\right)$ where $Z^{(i)}\sim N(0,1)$ i.i.d.
  2. Compute payoff for each path: $\Phi_i = \max(S_T^{(i)}-K, 0)$ (European call)
  3. Estimate price: $\hat{V} = e^{-rT}\,\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \Phi_i$
Standard error: $\text{SE} = e^{-rT}\,\dfrac{\text{std}(\Phi)}{\sqrt{N}}$

The MC error decreases as $O(1/\sqrt{N})$ — to halve the error, quadruple the number of paths.

Variance Reduction Techniques

TechniqueIdeaTypical VR factor
Antithetic variatesFor each $Z^{(i)}$, also use $-Z^{(i)}$. Average the two payoffs. Exploits negative correlation.2–10×
Control variatesUse a correlated quantity with known expectation (e.g.\ stock price under $\mathbb{Q}$) to reduce variance of the estimator.10–100×

Quasi-Monte CarloUse low-discrepancy sequences (Sobol, Halton) instead of pseudo-random numbers. Convergence $O(1/N)$ vs $O(1/\sqrt{N})$.10–100×

📈 When to Use Each Method
  • Closed-form BS: European vanilla options — always fastest and exact
  • Binomial tree: American options, discrete dividends, simple path-dependence
  • PDE finite difference: American options, local vol models, fast calibration
  • Monte Carlo: Exotic/path-dependent options (Asian, barrier, basket), multi-asset options, high-dimensional problems

The Greeks

The Greeks measure the sensitivity of an option's price to changes in its underlying parameters. They are the partial derivatives of the option pricing function and the primary tools for risk management.

All formulas below assume European options under Black-Scholes with $d_1$ and $d_2$ as defined in §1.3. $\phi(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}$ denotes the standard normal PDF.

2.1 Delta Δ — Price Sensitivity to Underlying

Delta Δ = ∂V/∂S

$$\Delta_\text{call} = \Phi(d_1) \qquad \Delta_\text{put} = \Phi(d_1) - 1 = -\Phi(-d_1)$$

Range: Call delta $\in (0,1)$; Put delta $\in (-1,0)$.

Interpretation: $\Delta$ is approximately the number of shares of stock needed to replicate the option. A delta-0.63 call behaves like holding 0.63 shares.

At key moneyness levels:

  • Deep ITM call: $\Delta \to 1$ (behaves like stock)
  • ATM call: $\Delta \approx 0.5$ (slightly above due to the $+\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2T$ in $d_1$)
  • Deep OTM call: $\Delta \to 0$

Also: $\Phi(d_2)$ = risk-neutral probability of expiring ITM. $\Delta = \Phi(d_1) \neq \Phi(d_2)$ (they differ by the volatility drag term).

✓ Relationship between call and put delta
$\Delta_\text{call} - \Delta_\text{put} = 1$ (from put-call parity: differentiate $C - P = S - Ke^{-rT}$ with respect to $S$).

2.2 Gamma Γ — Convexity in the Underlying

Gamma Γ = ∂²V/∂S²

$$\Gamma = \frac{\phi(d_1)}{S\,\sigma\sqrt{T}}$$

Gamma is the same for calls and puts (from put-call parity). It is always non-negative — options are convex in the stock price.

Interpretation: $\Gamma$ measures how fast $\Delta$ changes as $S$ moves. A high-gamma option requires frequent rebalancing to stay delta-hedged.

Key features:

  • Gamma is maximised when the option is ATM and near expiry ($T\to0$)
  • Far from ATM or far from expiry: gamma is low
  • Gamma "spikes" near expiry for ATM options — dangerous for short-gamma positions
📈 Gamma vs Theta Trade-off
Long options (long gamma) benefit from large moves in either direction but pay theta (time decay) every day. Short options (short gamma) earn theta but are hurt by large moves. The BS PDE makes this precise: $\Theta = -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma - rS\Delta + rV$. For an ATM call near expiry, $\Theta \approx -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma$.

2.3 Theta Θ — Time Decay

Theta Θ = ∂V/∂t

$$\Theta_\text{call} = -\frac{S\,\phi(d_1)\,\sigma}{2\sqrt{T}} - rKe^{-rT}\Phi(d_2)$$ $$\Theta_\text{put} = -\frac{S\,\phi(d_1)\,\sigma}{2\sqrt{T}} + rKe^{-rT}\Phi(-d_2)$$

Sign: $\Theta_\text{call} < 0$ always (long calls lose value as time passes). $\Theta_\text{put}$ can be positive (deep ITM puts may gain value from interest rate effect).

Convention: Theta is usually quoted as loss per calendar day: divide by 365 (or 252 for trading days).

ATM example: $S=K=100$, $\sigma=20\%$, $T=1$yr, $r=5\%$: $\Theta = -6.41$/yr $= -\mathbf{0.0176}$/day.

Theta decay is non-linear in time: $\Theta \propto 1/\sqrt{T}$ near expiry — options lose time value fastest in the last few weeks.

2.4 Vega ν — Volatility Sensitivity

Vega ν = ∂V/∂σ

$$\mathcal{V} = S\,\phi(d_1)\,\sqrt{T}$$

Vega is the same for calls and puts (from put-call parity). Always positive — options always increase in value with volatility.

ATM example: $\mathcal{V} = 37.52$ for our standard example, meaning a 1% rise in $\sigma$ (from 20% to 21%) increases the call price by approximately $0.3752$.

Key features:

  • Vega is maximised for ATM options with long time to expiry ($\mathcal{V} \propto \sqrt{T}$)
  • Deep ITM or OTM options have low vega
  • Vega is a primary risk in options books — "vega risk" from changes in implied vol
⚠ Vega is not a Greek Letter
"Vega" is not actually a Greek letter — it was invented for convenience. The Greek letter often used in formal texts is $\kappa$ or $\nu$ (nu). Despite this, "vega" is universal in practice. Also note: vega in BS assumes a parallel shift in the vol surface — in reality, different strikes/maturities have different vega exposures.

2.5 Rho ρ — Interest Rate Sensitivity

Rho ρ = ∂V/∂r

$$\rho_\text{call} = KTe^{-rT}\Phi(d_2) \qquad \rho_\text{put} = -KTe^{-rT}\Phi(-d_2)$$

Sign: $\rho_\text{call} > 0$ (higher rates increase call value — stock borrowing is "cheaper" relative to holding cash). $\rho_\text{put} < 0$.

Magnitude: Rho is typically the smallest of the major Greeks for short-dated options. For long-dated options (LEAPS), rho becomes significant.

ATM example: $\rho_\text{call} = 53.23$, meaning a 1% rise in $r$ increases the call by $\approx 0.5323$.

Rho is often ignored for equity options but matters for: (1) long-dated options, (2) currencies (interest rate differential), (3) interest rate derivatives where rho is the primary risk.

2.6 Delta Hedging

Delta hedging is the continuous rebalancing of a portfolio to eliminate first-order exposure to movements in $S$.

The Delta-Hedged Portfolio

$$\Pi = V - \Delta \cdot S \qquad \text{(long option, short }\Delta\text{ shares)}$$ $$d\Pi = dV - \Delta\,dS$$

By Itô's lemma: $dV = \Theta\,dt + \Delta\,dS + \frac{1}{2}\Gamma(dS)^2$. Substituting and using $(dS)^2 = \sigma^2 S^2\,dt$:

$$d\Pi = \left(\Theta + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\Gamma\right)dt$$

The $dS$ terms cancel — the portfolio is instantaneously risk-free. No-arbitrage then forces $d\Pi = r\Pi\,dt$, leading directly to the BS PDE.

P&L Decomposition of a Delta-Hedged Position

Over a small time interval $\Delta t$ with stock move $\Delta S$, the P&L of a long option delta-hedged position is:

Gamma P&L = Realised Variance vs Implied Variance
$$\text{P\&L} \approx \frac{1}{2}\Gamma(\Delta S)^2 + \Theta\,\Delta t = \frac{1}{2}\Gamma S^2\left[\left(\frac{\Delta S}{S}\right)^2 - \sigma_\text{impl}^2\,\Delta t\right]$$

The trader profits when realised variance $(\Delta S/S)^2$ exceeds implied variance $\sigma_\text{impl}^2\,\Delta t$ and loses otherwise. This is the fundamental "long gamma" trade: buying options and delta-hedging profits if actual volatility exceeds what you paid for in implied vol.

💡 Vega vs Gamma: Two Ways to Trade Volatility
Vega trade: Buy options, wait for implied vol to rise, sell. Profit from a change in the market's vol expectation.
Gamma trade: Buy options, delta-hedge continuously, profit from large daily moves (realised vol above implied vol).
The key difference: vega profits from changes in implied vol; gamma profits from realised vol exceeding implied vol — even if implied vol stays constant.

Fixed Income & Interest Rates

3.1 Bond Pricing & Yield

Bond Price from Cash Flows

A bond pays a coupon $C$ at times $t_1, \ldots, t_n$ and a face value $F$ at maturity $T = t_n$. Its price is the present value of all cash flows:

$$P = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{C}{(1+y/m)^{m t_i}} + \frac{F}{(1+y/m)^{mT}}$$

where $y$ is the yield to maturity (YTM) and $m$ is the payment frequency (e.g.\ $m=2$ for semi-annual). If using continuously compounded rates:

$$P = \sum_{i=1}^{n} C_i\,e^{-r_i t_i} + F\,e^{-r_T T}$$

where $r_i$ is the spot rate for maturity $t_i$ (from the yield curve).

Yield to Maturity (YTM)

The YTM $y$ is the single discount rate that equates the bond price to the present value of all cash flows — it's the bond's internal rate of return (IRR). There is no closed form; solve numerically (e.g.\ Newton-Raphson or bisection).

📋 Worked Example (Verified)
Face=$1000$, coupon rate=$5\%$ (semi-annual, coupon=$25$), maturity=3yr, YTM=$4\%$:

$P = 25\sum_{t=1}^{6}\frac{1}{1.02^t} + \frac{1000}{1.02^6} = 25\times5.601 + \frac{1000}{1.1262} = 140.03 + 888.00 = \mathbf{1028.01}$

Bond trades at a premium to par (coupon rate 5% > YTM 4%). ✓

Price-Yield Relationship

  • Price and yield move in opposite directions (inverse relationship)
  • When coupon rate = YTM: bond trades at par ($P = F$)
  • When coupon rate > YTM: bond trades at a premium ($P > F$)
  • When coupon rate < YTM: bond trades at a discount ($P < F$)

3.2 Duration & Convexity

Macaulay Duration

The weighted average time to receive cash flows, where weights are the PV of each cash flow as a fraction of total price:

$$D_\text{Mac} = \frac{1}{P}\sum_{i=1}^{n} t_i \cdot PV(C_i) = \frac{1}{P}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{t_i \cdot C_i}{(1+y/m)^{m t_i}} + \frac{T \cdot F}{(1+y/m)^{mT}}\right]$$

For a zero-coupon bond: $D_\text{Mac} = T$ (all cash flow at maturity).

Modified Duration

The first-order price sensitivity to yield changes:

$$D_\text{mod} = \frac{D_\text{Mac}}{1 + y/m} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \frac{dP}{dy} = -D_\text{mod} \cdot P$$
Price Change Approximation (First Order)
$$\frac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -D_\text{mod} \cdot \Delta y$$

For a small parallel shift $\Delta y$ in yields, a bond with $D_\text{mod} = 5$ years loses approximately 5% of its value per 1% rise in rates.

DV01 (Dollar Value of a Basis Point)

$$\text{DV01} = -\frac{dP}{dy}\cdot 0.0001 = D_\text{mod} \cdot P \cdot 0.0001$$

DV01 is the dollar change in price for a 1 basis point (0.01%) change in yield. Primary risk measure for fixed income traders.

Convexity

Second-order correction to the duration approximation:

$$\text{Convexity} = \frac{1}{P}\frac{d^2P}{dy^2} = \frac{1}{P(1+y/m)^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{t_i(t_i+1/m)\,PV(C_i)}{1}$$
Price Change with Convexity Correction
$$\Delta P \approx -D_\text{mod} \cdot P \cdot \Delta y + \frac{1}{2}\cdot\text{Convexity}\cdot P\cdot(\Delta y)^2$$
📋 Duration + Convexity (Verified)
Our 3yr bond: $D_\text{Mac} = 2.826$yr, $D_\text{mod} = 2.770$yr, Convexity $= 9.31$.
For a +100bp (1%) yield rise:
Duration-only: $\Delta P \approx -2.770\times1028\times0.01 = -28.47$
Duration + Convexity: $\Delta P \approx -28.47 + \frac{1}{2}(9.31)(1028)(0.0001) = -28.47 + 0.48 = -28.00$
Actual: $\Delta P = -28.01$ ✓
💡 Convexity is Always Positive (for Plain Bonds)
Convexity is always positive for standard fixed-rate bonds — this means the price-yield curve is convex: bonds fall less than duration predicts when rates rise, and rise more than duration predicts when rates fall. This asymmetry is a desirable property and investors pay for it. Callable bonds and mortgage-backed securities can have negative convexity.

3.3 Interest Rate Models

Interest rate models describe the evolution of the short rate $r_t$ under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$.

Vasicek Model

Vasicek SDE
$$dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$$

$\theta$: long-run mean rate. $\kappa>0$: speed of mean reversion. $\sigma$: volatility of rate changes.

Solution: $r_t$ is Gaussian (OU process — see Section 5, §4.5):

$$r_t = \theta + (r_0 - \theta)e^{-\kappa t} + \sigma\int_0^t e^{-\kappa(t-s)}\,dW_s$$ $$E[r_t] = \theta + (r_0-\theta)e^{-\kappa t}, \qquad \text{Var}(r_t) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}(1-e^{-2\kappa t})$$

Zero-coupon bond price: Vasicek has a closed-form affine bond price $P(0,T) = A(T)e^{-B(T)r_0}$ where:

$$B(T) = \frac{1-e^{-\kappa T}}{\kappa}, \qquad \ln A(T) = \left(\theta - \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa^2}\right)(B(T)-T) - \frac{\sigma^2 B(T)^2}{4\kappa}$$
PropertyVasicek
Mean reversion✓ — rates revert to $\theta$
Negative rates possible✓ — a limitation
Closed-form bond prices✓ — affine structure
Fitting current term structure✗ — fits a model curve, not exact market
Number of parameters3 ($\kappa, \theta, \sigma$)

Hull-White Model

Hull-White SDE
$$dr_t = [\theta(t) - \kappa r_t]\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$$

The key difference from Vasicek: $\theta$ is replaced by a time-dependent function $\theta(t)$, chosen to exactly reproduce the observed yield curve (calibrate to market prices). This is the "one-factor Hull-White" model.

$\theta(t)$ is determined by:

$$\theta(t) = \frac{\partial f^M(0,t)}{\partial t} + \kappa f^M(0,t) + \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}(1-e^{-2\kappa t})$$

where $f^M(0,t)$ is the market-observed instantaneous forward rate at time 0 for maturity $t$.

📈 Vasicek vs Hull-White in Practice
Vasicek is pedagogically clean but cannot match today's yield curve exactly. Hull-White adds the time-dependent drift $\theta(t)$ to fit the current market term structure perfectly, making it the industry standard for short-rate modelling in pricing desks. Both share the convenient property that bond prices, caps, floors, and swaptions have closed-form solutions.

3.4 Swap Pricing Basics

An interest rate swap is an agreement to exchange fixed cash flows for floating cash flows (or vice versa) on a notional principal $N$ over a set of payment dates.

Structure

  • Fixed leg: Pays $N \cdot s \cdot \Delta t$ at each payment date, where $s$ is the fixed (swap) rate and $\Delta t$ is the accrual period
  • Floating leg: Pays $N \cdot L(t_{i-1}, t_i) \cdot \Delta t$ at $t_i$, where $L$ is the LIBOR/SOFR rate set at the start of each period

Pricing at Inception (Par Swap)

At inception, a fair swap has zero value. The swap rate $s$ is set so that:

$$\text{PV fixed leg} = \text{PV floating leg}$$ $$s \cdot \Delta t \sum_{i=1}^n P(0,t_i) = 1 - P(0,t_n)$$

This gives the swap rate:

Swap Rate Formula
$$s = \frac{1 - P(0,t_n)}{\Delta t \sum_{i=1}^n P(0,t_i)} = \frac{1 - \text{DF}(T)}{\text{Annuity factor}}$$

where $P(0,t_i)$ are zero-coupon discount factors (from the yield curve) and the denominator is the present value of \$1 per period (the "annuity factor").

Why PV floating = $1 - P(0,t_n)$: The floating leg replicates a strategy of investing \$1 now, rolling it at LIBOR each period, and receiving the interest at each payment date. At time $t_n$ you recover \$1 (the principal). So the PV of all floating payments equals $1 - P(0,t_n)$ (the current price of \$1 at $t_n$).
📋 Worked Example (Verified)
3yr annual swap, spot rates: 1yr=3%, 2yr=4%, 3yr=5%.
Discount factors: $P(0,1)=0.9709$, $P(0,2)=0.9246$, $P(0,3)=0.8638$.

Annuity = $0.9709+0.9246+0.8638 = 2.7593$
Swap rate $= \dfrac{1 - 0.8638}{2.7593} = \dfrac{0.1362}{2.7593} = \mathbf{4.93\%}$

Intuition: upward-sloping curve (3-5%) ⟹ swap rate (4.93%) lies between the 1yr and 3yr spot rates. ✓

Mark-to-Market Value of an Existing Swap

For a pay-fixed, receive-floating swap with remaining payment dates $t_1,\ldots,t_n$ and fixed rate $s_0$ (agreed at inception):

$$V_\text{swap} = (s_\text{current} - s_0)\cdot \Delta t \cdot N \cdot \sum_{i=1}^n P(\text{now},t_i)$$

If current rates have risen (swap rate $s_\text{current} > s_0$), the fixed receiver loses value; if rates have fallen, they gain.
