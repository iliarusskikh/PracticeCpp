Markov Chains

A discrete-time stochastic process $\{X_n\}$ has the Markov property if the future depends on the past only through the present:

$$P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i, X_{n-1}, \ldots, X_0) = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i)$$

"Memorylessness at the level of the whole state" — this is the discrete-state, discrete-time analogue of the memoryless property seen in the Exponential and Geometric distributions (Section 2).

1.1 Transition Matrix & Chapman-Kolmogorov

For a chain with state space $\{1, \ldots, k\}$, the transition matrix $P$ has entries:

$$P_{ij} = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i), \qquad \sum_{j} P_{ij} = 1 \;\;\text{(each row sums to 1)}$$

Chapman-Kolmogorov Equation

The $n$-step transition probabilities are given by matrix powers:

$$P^{(n)}_{ij} = (P^n)_{ij}, \qquad P^{(m+n)}_{ij} = \sum_k P^{(m)}_{ik}P^{(n)}_{kj}$$

In words: to go from $i$ to $j$ in $m+n$ steps, pass through some intermediate state $k$ at time $m$ — sum over all possible intermediate states. This is literally the matrix multiplication rule.

1.2 Stationary Distribution π

A distribution $\pi$ (row vector, $\pi_i \geq 0$, $\sum_i \pi_i = 1$) is stationary if it is unchanged by one step of the chain:

$$\pi P = \pi$$

Equivalently, $\pi$ is the left eigenvector of $P$ for eigenvalue 1 (normalised to sum to 1). Every finite, irreducible Markov chain has a unique stationary distribution.

How to Find π — Three Equivalent Methods

  1. Solve the linear system: $\pi P = \pi$ together with $\sum_i \pi_i = 1$ — equivalent to $\pi(P - I) = 0$
  2. Left eigenvector: Find the eigenvector of $P^\top$ for eigenvalue $\lambda = 1$, normalise to sum to 1
  3. Power iteration: Compute $P^n$ for large $n$ — every row converges to $\pi$ (works when the chain is ergodic)
📋 Worked Example
$P = \begin{pmatrix}0.7&0.2&0.1\\0.3&0.5&0.2\\0.2&0.3&0.5\end{pmatrix}$

Solving $\pi P = \pi$, $\sum\pi_i=1$ gives $\pi \approx (0.463,\ 0.317,\ 0.220)$.

Verified three ways (linear solve, eigenvector, and $P^{200}$ row) — all agree to 4 decimal places.

1.3 Detailed Balance

A stronger, easier-to-check sufficient condition for stationarity:

Detailed Balance (Reversibility)
$$\pi_i P_{ij} = \pi_j P_{ji} \qquad \text{for all } i, j$$
Why detailed balance implies stationarity: Sum both sides over $i$: $\sum_i \pi_i P_{ij} = \sum_i \pi_j P_{ji} = \pi_j \sum_i P_{ji} = \pi_j$. The left side is exactly $(\pi P)_j$, so $\pi P = \pi$. ✓

Detailed balance is sufficient but not necessary — a chain can be stationary without being reversible (e.g.\ chains with directional "circulation," like a cyclic random walk). Detailed balance always holds for birth-death chains (chains that only move to adjacent states, like a 1-D random walk on $\{0,\ldots,N\}$).

💡 Physical Interpretation
Detailed balance says: the "flow" of probability from $i\to j$ exactly equals the flow from $j\to i$ for every pair — a much stronger, microscopic condition than the macroscopic balance $\pi P = \pi$ (which only requires total flow in = total flow out for each state). It's the discrete analogue of thermodynamic equilibrium.

1.4 Absorbing States

A state $i$ is absorbing if $P_{ii} = 1$ (once entered, never left). A chain with both absorbing and transient states can be reordered into canonical block form:

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} Q & R \\ 0 & I \end{pmatrix}$$ $Q$: transient→transient, $R$: transient→absorbing, $I$: absorbing states stay put

The Fundamental Matrix

$$N = (I - Q)^{-1}$$

$N_{ij}$ = expected number of visits to transient state $j$ starting from transient state $i$, before absorption.

QuantityFormulaInterpretation
Expected time to absorption$\mathbf{t} = N\mathbf{1}$ (row sums of $N$)Total expected steps before absorption
Absorption probabilities$B = NR$$B_{ij}$ = probability of absorption in state $j$, starting from $i$
📋 Worked Example: Gambler's Ruin as Absorbing Chain
Fair gambler's ruin, $N=3$ (states 0, 1, 2, 3; states 0 and 3 absorbing). Transient states $\{1,2\}$:

$Q = \begin{pmatrix}0&0.5\\0.5&0\end{pmatrix}$, $\;N = (I-Q)^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1.333&0.667\\0.667&1.333\end{pmatrix}$

Expected steps to absorption: $\mathbf{t} = N\mathbf{1} = (2, 2)$ — matches the formula $E[T\mid k] = k(N-k)$: $1\times2=2$, $2\times1=2$ ✓

Absorption probabilities: $B = NR = \begin{pmatrix}0.667 & 0.333\\0.333&0.667\end{pmatrix}$ — matches $P(\text{absorb at }N\mid k)=k/N$: $1/3, 2/3$ ✓

1.5 Hitting Times & Gambler's Ruin

The hitting time $T_A = \min\{n \geq 0 : X_n \in A\}$ is the first time the chain enters set $A$. The standard tool is first-step analysis: condition on the first transition.

First-Step Analysis for Expected Hitting Time
$$h_i = E[T_A \mid X_0 = i] = 1 + \sum_j P_{ij}\,h_j \quad (i \notin A), \qquad h_i = 0 \;(i \in A)$$

This recovers the Gambler's Ruin results from Section 1 (Probability) using the Markov chain machinery: with $P_{i,i+1}=p$, $P_{i,i-1}=q$ on states $\{0,\ldots,N\}$ with $0,N$ absorbing:

$$P(\text{absorb at }N \mid X_0=k) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{k}{N} & p=q=\tfrac{1}{2} \\[0.4em] \dfrac{1-(q/p)^k}{1-(q/p)^N} & p \neq q\end{cases} \qquad E[T \mid X_0=k] = k(N-k) \;\;(p=q=\tfrac12)$$
✓ First-Step Analysis Recipe
  1. Define $h_i$ = quantity of interest starting from state $i$ (hitting probability or expected time)
  2. Write the recursive equation conditioning on the first transition
  3. Apply boundary conditions at absorbing states
  4. Solve the resulting linear recurrence (often a simple difference equation)
This same technique solves hitting probabilities for any birth-death chain, not just symmetric gambler's ruin.

1.6 Ergodic Chains

A chain is ergodic if it is irreducible (all states communicate) and aperiodic (no forced cyclic structure).

Ergodic Theorem
For an ergodic chain with stationary distribution $\pi$: $$\lim_{n\to\infty} P^{(n)}_{ij} = \pi_j \quad \text{for all } i, j \;\;\text{(independent of starting state)}$$ $$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} \mathbf{1}[X_t = j] \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \pi_j \quad \text{(time average = ensemble average)}$$

Why ergodicity matters: the long-run fraction of time spent in state $j$ equals $\pi_j$ — regardless of starting state. This justifies simulating one long path instead of many independent short paths to estimate $\pi$ (used in MCMC).

⚠ Periodicity Breaks Convergence (not Stationarity)
A periodic chain (e.g.\ a 2-cycle $0\leftrightarrow1$ with $P_{01}=P_{10}=1$) still has a unique stationary distribution $\pi=(0.5,0.5)$, but $P^{(n)}$ does not converge to $\pi$ — it oscillates between $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ forever. Stationarity (existence of $\pi$) and convergence of $P^n$ are different properties — irreducibility + aperiodicity is needed for the latter.

1.7 Random Walks on Graphs

For a connected, undirected graph $G=(V,E)$, the simple random walk moves to a uniformly random neighbour at each step: $P_{ij} = \frac{1}{\deg(i)}$ if $(i,j)\in E$.

Stationary Distribution: Proportional to Degree
$$\pi_i = \frac{\deg(i)}{2|E|}$$
Proof via detailed balance: Check $\pi_i P_{ij} = \frac{\deg(i)}{2|E|}\cdot\frac{1}{\deg(i)} = \frac{1}{2|E|}$. By symmetry this equals $\pi_j P_{ji} = \frac{1}{2|E|}$ whenever $(i,j) \in E$. Detailed balance holds, so $\pi$ is stationary (and the walk is reversible).

This connects graph theory directly to Markov chains: PageRank is a (damped) random walk on the web graph; the stationary distribution gives the page ranking.

Expected Return Time

$$E[\text{return time to } i] = \frac{1}{\pi_i} = \frac{2|E|}{\deg(i)}$$

High-degree nodes ("hubs") are revisited more frequently — intuitive, since the walk is more likely to step into them.

Brownian Motion

2.1 Definition & Basic Properties

Standard Brownian motion (Wiener process) $\{W_t\}_{t\geq0}$ is the continuous-time, continuous-path limit of a random walk. It is defined by four properties:

Defining Properties of Standard Brownian Motion
  1. $W_0 = 0$
  2. Independent increments: for $0\leq s
  3. Stationary Gaussian increments: $W_t - W_s \sim N(0,\, t-s)$
  4. Continuous sample paths (almost surely)

From property 3 with $s=0$: $W_t \sim N(0, t)$ — the defining marginal distribution.

Moments

QuantityFormula
$E[W_t]$$0$
$\text{Var}(W_t)$$t$
$\text{Cov}(W_s, W_t)$$\min(s,t)$
$\text{Corr}(W_s,W_t)$$\sqrt{s/t}$ for $s
Proof of $\text{Cov}(W_s,W_t)=\min(s,t)$ for $s Write $W_t = W_s + (W_t-W_s)$. Then $\text{Cov}(W_s,W_t) = \text{Cov}(W_s,W_s) + \text{Cov}(W_s, W_t-W_s) = \text{Var}(W_s) + 0 = s$ (using independent increments). In general: $\text{Cov}(W_s,W_t) = \min(s,t)$.

Self-Similarity (Scaling Property)

For any $c>0$: $\quad \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{c}}W_{ct} \stackrel{d}{=} W_t \quad$ (same distribution as a process)

Brownian motion "looks the same" at any time scale after rescaling — the origin of fractal-like path roughness, and the reason volatility scales as $\sqrt{T}$ in finance ("volatility scaling law").

2.2 Independent & Stationary Increments

Independent increments: non-overlapping time intervals give independent changes. This means $W_{t_2}-W_{t_1}$ and $W_{t_4}-W_{t_3}$ are independent for $t_1

Stationary increments: the distribution of $W_{t+h}-W_t$ depends only on $h$, not on $t$ itself: $W_{t+h}-W_t \sim N(0,h)$ for any $t$.

💡 Why This Matters for Pricing
Independent increments make Brownian motion a building block for modelling "shocks" that don't depend on history — consistent with weak-form market efficiency (future price changes are unpredictable from past changes). Stationary increments mean the statistical behaviour of returns doesn't depend on calendar time — the foundation of constant-volatility models like Black-Scholes.

2.3 Quadratic Variation — The Key to Itô Calculus

For a partition $0=t_0quadratic variation is:

Quadratic Variation of Brownian Motion
$$[W,W]_t = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n (W_{t_i}-W_{t_{i-1}})^2 = t \quad \text{(almost surely)}$$
Sketch of proof: Let $\Delta_i = W_{t_i}-W_{t_{i-1}} \sim N(0,\Delta t_i)$ independently. Then $E\left[\sum \Delta_i^2\right] = \sum \Delta t_i = t$. The variance of the sum $\to 0$ as mesh $\to 0$ (since $\text{Var}(\Delta_i^2) = 2(\Delta t_i)^2$, and $\sum(\Delta t_i)^2 \to 0$ when $\sum\Delta t_i = t$ stays fixed but mesh shrinks). So the sum concentrates at its mean, $t$.

Contrast with ordinary calculus: for any differentiable function $f$, the quadratic variation is 0 (sum of squared increments vanishes as mesh $\to 0$, since each $\Delta_i = O(\Delta t_i)$, giving $\Delta_i^2 = O((\Delta t_i)^2)\to 0$ faster than $\Delta t_i$). For Brownian motion, $\Delta_i = O(\sqrt{\Delta t_i})$ — paths are "rough" — so $\Delta_i^2 = O(\Delta t_i)$, the same order as $\Delta t_i$, giving a non-zero limit.

The Heuristic "Itô Multiplication Rule" — Memorise This
$$dW \cdot dW = dt \qquad dW \cdot dt = 0 \qquad dt \cdot dt = 0$$

This single fact — $(dW)^2 = dt$ — is the entire reason stochastic calculus differs from ordinary calculus and is the engine behind Itô's Lemma (§3.2).

Total variation (sum of $|\Delta_i|$, not squared) of Brownian motion is infinite on any interval — Brownian paths are continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere.

2.4 Reflection Principle

Let $M_t = \max_{0\leq s\leq t} W_s$ be the running maximum. The reflection principle relates the distribution of the maximum to the distribution of the endpoint:

Reflection Principle
$$P(M_t \geq a) = 2\,P(W_t \geq a) \qquad \text{for } a > 0$$
Proof sketch: By the strong Markov property, once $W$ hits level $a$ (at the first hitting time $\tau_a$), the path $W_{\tau_a + s} - a$ is itself a Brownian motion, independent of the past, and symmetric around 0. So paths that hit $a$ and end above $a$ are exactly as likely as paths that hit $a$ and end below $a$ (reflect the post-hitting path). Therefore: $$P(W_t \geq a) = P(M_t \geq a, W_t \geq a) = \tfrac{1}{2}P(M_t \geq a)$$ since conditional on $M_t \geq a$, by symmetry of the reflected path, $W_t \geq a$ and $W_t < a$ are equally likely. Rearranging gives the result.

Distribution of the maximum:

$$P(M_t \geq a) = 2\left(1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{t}}\right)\right) = 2P(W_t \geq a)$$
📈 Finance: Barrier Option Pricing
The reflection principle is the core tool for pricing barrier options (knock-in/knock-out) and lookback options. The price of a down-and-out call depends on $P(\min_{0\leq s\leq T} S_s > B)$ for barrier $B$ — computed by mapping to Brownian motion via $\ln S_t$ and applying reflection.

2.5 Martingales

A process $\{X_t\}$ is a martingale with respect to filtration $\mathcal{F}_t$ if:

$$E[X_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = X_s \qquad \text{for all } s < t \qquad \text{(and } E[|X_t|]<\infty\text{)}$$

Intuition: "fair game" — your best prediction of the future value, given everything known now, is exactly the current value. No systematic drift.

Key Martingales Built from Brownian Motion

ProcessMartingale?Proof Idea
$W_t$✓ Yes$E[W_t\mid\mathcal{F}_s] = W_s + E[W_t-W_s\mid\mathcal{F}_s] = W_s+0$
$W_t^2 - t$✓ Yes$E[W_t^2\mid\mathcal{F}_s] = W_s^2+(t-s)$, so $E[W_t^2-t\mid\mathcal{F}_s]=W_s^2-s$
$e^{\sigma W_t - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t}$✓ Yes (exponential martingale)Uses MGF of normal: $E[e^{\sigma(W_t-W_s)}] = e^{\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2(t-s)}$
Discounted stock $e^{-rt}S_t$ (under $\mathbb{Q}$)✓ YesRisk-neutral pricing requires this by construction
💡 The Exponential Martingale — Foundation of Girsanov
$$Z_t = \exp\!\left(\sigma W_t - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t\right), \qquad E[Z_t] = 1 \text{ for all } t$$ Because $E[Z_t]=1$ always, $Z_t$ can serve as a valid Radon-Nikodym derivative for a measure change — this is exactly the building block of Girsanov's theorem (§3.5).

Why Martingales Matter in Finance

The Fundamental Theorem of Asset Pricing: a market is arbitrage-free if and only if there exists an equivalent martingale measure $\mathbb{Q}$ under which discounted asset prices are martingales. Pricing a derivative reduces to taking an expectation under $\mathbb{Q}$:

$$V_0 = E^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[e^{-rT}\,\text{Payoff}(S_T)\right]$$

Itô Calculus

3.1 The Itô Integral

For an adapted process $f_t$, the Itô integral $\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t$ is constructed as a limit of left-endpoint Riemann sums:

$$\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f_{t_i}\,(W_{t_{i+1}}-W_{t_i})$$

Crucial detail: the integrand $f_{t_i}$ is evaluated at the left endpoint $t_i$, not the right endpoint or midpoint — this choice (non-anticipating / adapted) is what makes the Itô integral a martingale and is essential for the no-arbitrage interpretation: you cannot use future information to decide your current trading position.

Key Properties

PropertyFormula
Zero mean$E\left[\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t\right] = 0$
Itô isometry$E\left[\left(\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t\right)^2\right] = E\left[\int_0^T f_t^2\,dt\right]$
Martingale$M_t = \int_0^t f_s\,dW_s$ is a martingale
💡 Itô Isometry — Why It's Useful
The isometry converts a stochastic integral's variance into an ordinary (deterministic) time integral — this is how variance of complex SDE solutions is computed without ever simulating the path. For example, $\text{Var}\left(\int_0^T \sigma\,dW_t\right) = \sigma^2 T$.

3.2 Itô's Lemma

The single most important formula in quantitative finance. Suppose $X_t$ follows the SDE $dX_t = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$, and let $f(X_t,t)$ be a twice-differentiable function. Then:

Itô's Lemma (one dimension)
$$df = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \mu\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\right)dt + \sigma\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\,dW_t$$
Derivation via Taylor expansion + Itô multiplication table: Taylor-expand $f(X+dX, t+dt)$ to second order: $$df = f_t\,dt + f_x\,dX + \tfrac{1}{2}f_{xx}(dX)^2 + \ldots$$ Substitute $dX = \mu\,dt+\sigma\,dW$ and expand $(dX)^2 = \mu^2(dt)^2 + 2\mu\sigma\,dt\,dW + \sigma^2(dW)^2$. Apply the multiplication table: $(dt)^2=0$, $dt\,dW=0$, $(dW)^2=dt$. So $(dX)^2 \to \sigma^2\,dt$. Substituting back gives Itô's Lemma. The extra $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f_{xx}\,dt$ term is what makes stochastic calculus different from ordinary calculus — in ordinary calculus this term would vanish.

Multivariate Itô's Lemma

For $f(X_t, Y_t, t)$ with two correlated diffusions $dX = \mu_X dt + \sigma_X dW_1$, $dY = \mu_Y dt + \sigma_Y dW_2$, $dW_1\,dW_2 = \rho\,dt$:

$$df = f_t\,dt + f_x\,dX + f_y\,dY + \tfrac{1}{2}f_{xx}\sigma_X^2\,dt + \tfrac{1}{2}f_{yy}\sigma_Y^2\,dt + f_{xy}\,\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y\,dt$$
📋 Classic Application: $d(\ln S_t)$ for GBM
Let $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$ and $f(S) = \ln S$. Then $f_S = 1/S$, $f_{SS} = -1/S^2$.

$$d(\ln S) = \left(0 + \mu S\cdot\frac{1}{S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\cdot\left(-\frac{1}{S^2}\right)\right)dt + \sigma S\cdot\frac{1}{S}\,dW$$ $$d(\ln S) = \left(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)dt + \sigma\,dW$$ The famous "$-\sigma^2/2$" correction — the log-price drifts slower than the naive $\mu$ because of volatility drag (Jensen's inequality, Section 2).

3.3 Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)

GBM SDE
$$dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t$$

Solving the SDE: from $d(\ln S) = (\mu-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)dt + \sigma\,dW$ (just derived), integrate both sides from 0 to $t$:

Closed-Form Solution
$$S_t = S_0\exp\!\left(\left(\mu-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W_t\right)$$

$S_t$ is log-normally distributed (Section 2): $\ln S_t \sim N\!\left(\ln S_0 + (\mu-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)t,\ \sigma^2 t\right)$.

QuantityFormulaNote
$E[S_t]$$S_0 e^{\mu t}$Uses Jensen / log-normal mean formula: NOT $S_0e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t}$
$\text{Median}(S_t)$$S_0 e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t}$The "typical" path; below the mean
$\text{Var}(S_t)$$S_0^2 e^{2\mu t}(e^{\sigma^2 t}-1)$From log-normal variance formula
⚠ Mean vs Median — Classic Interview Trap
A simulated GBM path almost always ends up below $S_0e^{\mu t}$ because the median ($S_0e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t}$) is what a "typical" path looks like, but the mean is pulled up by a small probability of extremely large outcomes (right-skewed log-normal). This is exactly volatility drag from Jensen's inequality.

3.4 SDEs: Drift & Diffusion

The general stochastic differential equation:

$$dX_t = \underbrace{\mu(X_t,t)}_{\text{drift}}\,dt + \underbrace{\sigma(X_t,t)}_{\text{diffusion}}\,dW_t$$

Drift $\mu(X,t)$: deterministic component, "where the process tends to go on average."
Diffusion $\sigma(X,t)$: scales the randomness, controls the magnitude of fluctuations.

Catalogue of Important SDEs in Finance

ModelSDEUse
Brownian motion with drift$dX = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW$Simplest model; arithmetic returns
GBM$dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$Black-Scholes stock price model
Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (Vasicek)$dX = \theta(\mu-X)dt + \sigma\,dW$Mean-reverting rates, pairs trading
CIR process$dX = \theta(\mu-X)dt + \sigma\sqrt{X}\,dW$Non-negative rates / variance (Heston vol)
Jump-diffusion (Merton)$dS/S = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW + dJ$Models crashes / discontinuities

Existence & Uniqueness

Solutions exist and are unique if $\mu, \sigma$ satisfy Lipschitz and linear growth conditions — virtually always true for the models used in practice. The CIR process needs the Feller condition $2\theta\mu \geq \sigma^2$ to stay strictly positive.

3.5 Girsanov's Theorem

Girsanov's theorem describes how Brownian motion transforms under a change of probability measure — the mathematical machinery behind risk-neutral pricing.

Girsanov's Theorem (constant drift case)
Let $W_t$ be Brownian motion under measure $\mathbb{P}$. Define a new measure $\mathbb{Q}$ via the Radon-Nikodym derivative: $$\frac{d\mathbb{Q}}{d\mathbb{P}} = \exp\!\left(-\theta W_T - \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^2 T\right)$$ Then under $\mathbb{Q}$, the process $\tilde{W}_t = W_t + \theta t$ is a standard Brownian motion.

In words: a Brownian motion with drift under one measure is a driftless Brownian motion under another (equivalent) measure — you can "shift away" the drift by reweighting probabilities, while the volatility $\sigma$ is unchanged.

Application: From Real-World to Risk-Neutral Measure

Under the real-world (physical) measure $\mathbb{P}$: $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW^{\mathbb{P}}$.

Choose $\theta = \frac{\mu - r}{\sigma}$ (the market price of risk). By Girsanov, $W_t^{\mathbb{Q}} = W_t^{\mathbb{P}} + \theta t$ is $\mathbb{Q}$-Brownian motion, and:

$$dS = r S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW^{\mathbb{Q}} \qquad \text{(risk-neutral dynamics)}$$
💡 Why This Is the Engine of Derivative Pricing
Girsanov lets us replace the real-world drift $\mu$ (unobservable, requires estimating risk premia) with the risk-free rate $r$ (observable) — without changing the volatility $\sigma$. This is why Black-Scholes option prices don't depend on $\mu$: it gets "Girsanov'd away," leaving only $\sigma$, $r$, $S$, $K$, $T$ as inputs.

3.6 Feynman-Kac Formula

The Feynman-Kac formula is the bridge between PDEs and expectations — it formalises why solving the Black-Scholes PDE (Calculus, Section 4) is equivalent to computing a risk-neutral expectation.

Feynman-Kac Theorem
Suppose $V(x,t)$ solves the PDE: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \mu(x,t)\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2(x,t)\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial x^2} - r\,V = 0, \qquad V(x,T) = \Phi(x)$$ Then $V(x,t)$ has the probabilistic representation: $$V(x,t) = E\left[e^{-r(T-t)}\,\Phi(X_T) \;\middle|\; X_t = x\right]$$ where $X_t$ follows $dX = \mu(X,t)\,dt + \sigma(X,t)\,dW$ under the expectation measure.
💡 Feynman-Kac ⟺ Black-Scholes
Apply Feynman-Kac with $\mu(x,t)=rx$ (risk-neutral drift), $\sigma(x,t)=\sigma x$, and terminal condition $\Phi(S) = \max(S-K,0)$ (call payoff). This recovers exactly the Black-Scholes PDE from the Calculus section, and confirms: $$C(S,t) = E^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[e^{-r(T-t)}\max(S_T-K,0) \mid S_t=S\right]$$ This is precisely how the Black-Scholes formula is derived via the risk-neutral expectation route (as opposed to the delta-hedging PDE route) — both arrive at the same answer because of Feynman-Kac.

The Two Equivalent Routes to Option Pricing

4.1 AR, MA, ARMA, ARIMA Models

Autoregressive: AR(p)

$$X_t = c + \phi_1 X_{t-1} + \phi_2 X_{t-2} + \cdots + \phi_p X_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \text{WN}(0,\sigma^2)$$

Current value depends linearly on its own past values plus white noise. AR(1): $X_t = c + \phi X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$ — the workhorse model; stationary iff $|\phi|<1$.

Moving Average: MA(q)

$$X_t = \mu + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1\varepsilon_{t-1} + \cdots + \theta_q\varepsilon_{t-q}$$

Current value is a linear combination of current and past shocks (not past values of $X$ itself). MA processes are always stationary regardless of $\theta$ values (finite sum of stationary terms) — unlike AR processes.

ARMA(p,q)

$$X_t = c + \sum_{i=1}^p \phi_i X_{t-i} + \varepsilon_t + \sum_{j=1}^q \theta_j \varepsilon_{t-j}$$

Combines both: autoregressive part captures persistence, moving-average part captures shock propagation. An AR($\infty$) process can often be well-approximated by a low-order ARMA($p$,$q$) — parsimony.

ARIMA(p,d,q)

For non-stationary series, difference $d$ times until stationary, then fit ARMA(p,q) to the differenced series:

$$(1-L)^d X_t \sim \text{ARMA}(p,q) \qquad \text{where } L \text{ is the lag operator: } LX_t = X_{t-1}$$

$d=1$ (first differencing) handles a unit root / stochastic trend — the most common case for asset prices, which are typically I(1) (integrated of order 1) while log-returns are I(0) (stationary).

4.2 Stationarity & Unit Roots

Weak (Covariance) Stationarity

A process is weakly stationary if:
  1. $E[X_t] = \mu$ (constant, independent of $t$)
  2. $\text{Var}(X_t) = \sigma^2 < \infty$ (constant, finite)
  3. $\text{Cov}(X_t, X_{t+k}) = \gamma(k)$ depends only on lag $k$, not on $t$

AR(1) Stationarity Condition

For $X_t = \phi X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$: stationary iff $|\phi| < 1$. The stationary (unconditional) variance is:

$$\text{Var}(X_t) = \frac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{1-\phi^2}$$

As $\phi \to 1$, variance blows up — the boundary case $\phi=1$ is a random walk ($X_t = X_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t$), which is non-stationary (variance grows linearly in $t$: $\text{Var}(X_t) = t\sigma^2$).

Unit Root Testing

$\phi = 1$ is called a unit root (the characteristic equation $1-\phi z = 0$ has root $z=1$). The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test tests $H_0: \phi=1$ (unit root, non-stationary) vs $H_1: |\phi|<1$ (stationary):

$$\Delta X_t = \alpha + \beta t + \gamma X_{t-1} + \sum_{i=1}^p \delta_i \Delta X_{t-i} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad H_0: \gamma = 0$$

Note the test statistic does not follow a standard $t$-distribution under $H_0$ — special Dickey-Fuller critical values are needed (the distribution is skewed due to the non-stationarity under the null).

📈 Finance: Why This Matters for Pairs Trading
A pairs-trading strategy bets that the spread between two cointegrated assets is stationary (mean-reverting) even though each individual price series has a unit root (is non-stationary). The Engle-Granger two-step method: (1) regress one price on the other, (2) ADF-test the residuals for stationarity. If residuals are stationary, the pair is cointegrated and tradeable.

4.4 Volatility Clustering / GARCH

Volatility clustering: large price changes tend to be followed by large changes (of either sign), and small changes by small changes — variance is autocorrelated even when returns themselves are not (a key stylised fact of financial returns).

ARCH(1) — Building Block

$$r_t = \sigma_t \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \text{i.i.d. } N(0,1), \qquad \sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha\,r_{t-1}^2$$

GARCH(1,1) — The Industry Standard

GARCH(1,1) Variance Equation
$$\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha\,r_{t-1}^2 + \beta\,\sigma_{t-1}^2$$

$\omega>0$, $\alpha,\beta\geq0$, and stationarity requires $\alpha+\beta<1$.

📋 Verified Simulation: GARCH(1,1), $\omega=0.05,\alpha=0.10,\beta=0.85$
Persistence $\alpha+\beta = 0.95$ (close to 1: realistic high persistence as seen in real markets).
Theoretical unconditional variance: $\omega/(1-\alpha-\beta) = 1.00$; simulated: $1.009$ — matches.
ACF of squared returns: lag 1 = 0.160, lag 5 = 0.138, lag 10 = 0.108, lag 20 = 0.065 — all positive and slowly decaying, confirming volatility clustering (returns themselves have ACF ≈ 0, but squared returns are autocorrelated).

Forecasting Volatility with GARCH

$$E_t[\sigma_{t+h}^2] = \sigma^2 + (\alpha+\beta)^{h-1}(\sigma_{t+1}^2-\sigma^2)$$

Volatility forecasts mean-revert geometrically to the long-run level $\sigma^2$, at rate $(\alpha+\beta)$ — directly analogous to the AR(1) and OU mean-reversion structure.

📈 Finance: GARCH vs Implied Volatility
GARCH models volatility from the historical return time series (statistical/physical measure). Implied volatility comes from option prices (risk-neutral measure) via inverting Black-Scholes. They generally differ — implied vol embeds a variance risk premium, typically running above GARCH-forecasted realised volatility, especially before known events.

4.5 Mean Reversion: The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) Process

The OU process is the continuous-time limit of AR(1) — the natural model for mean-reverting quantities (interest rates, volatility, spreads, pairs-trading residuals).

OU Process SDE
$$dX_t = \theta(\mu - X_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$$

$\mu$: long-run mean. $\theta>0$: speed of mean reversion. $\sigma$: volatility.

Closed-Form Solution

$$X_t = \mu + (X_0-\mu)e^{-\theta t} + \sigma\int_0^t e^{-\theta(t-s)}\,dW_s$$

$X_t$ is Gaussian (since it's a linear functional of $W$):

QuantityFormula
$E[X_t]$$\mu + (X_0-\mu)e^{-\theta t} \;\to\; \mu$ as $t\to\infty$
$\text{Var}(X_t)$$\dfrac{\sigma^2}{2\theta}\left(1-e^{-2\theta t}\right) \;\to\; \dfrac{\sigma^2}{2\theta}$ as $t\to\infty$
Stationary distribution$X_\infty \sim N\!\left(\mu,\ \dfrac{\sigma^2}{2\theta}\right)$
Half-life of mean reversion$\dfrac{\ln 2}{\theta}$
📋 Verified Simulation: $\theta=2,\ \mu=0.05,\ \sigma=0.1$
After long time $T=50$: simulated mean $=0.048$ (theory $\mu=0.05$), simulated variance $=0.00243$ (theory $\sigma^2/2\theta = 0.0025$). Matches closely.

OU as the Continuous Limit of AR(1)

Discretise the OU SDE with step $\Delta t$: $X_{t+\Delta t} - X_t \approx \theta(\mu-X_t)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,\varepsilon_t$, giving:

$$X_{t+\Delta t} = \underbrace{(1-\theta\Delta t)}_{\phi}X_t + \theta\mu\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,\varepsilon_t$$

This is exactly AR(1) with $\phi = 1-\theta\Delta t$. As $\Delta t \to 0$ with $\theta$ fixed, $\phi\to 1$ from below — connecting discrete AR(1) persistence directly to continuous mean-reversion speed.

📈 Finance: Where OU Shows Up
  • Vasicek interest rate model: $dr_t = \theta(\mu-r_t)dt+\sigma dW_t$ — exactly OU; rates mean-revert to long-run level $\mu$
  • Pairs trading: the spread between two cointegrated stocks is modelled as OU; entry/exit thresholds set in units of $\sigma/\sqrt{2\theta}$ (the stationary standard deviation)
  • Heston / SABR stochastic volatility: variance often follows a (square-root-modified) OU-type mean-reverting process
  • Optimal trading speed: in Almgren-Chriss style execution problems, alpha signals are often modelled as OU processes decaying at rate $\theta$
