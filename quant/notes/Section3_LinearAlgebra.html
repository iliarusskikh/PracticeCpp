Core Operations

1.1 Matrix Multiplication & Dot Product

Dot Product

For vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i = \|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the vectors. The geometric interpretation is fundamental: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0 \Leftrightarrow$ the vectors are orthogonal.

Matrix Multiplication

For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times k}$ and $B \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times n}$, the product $C = AB \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ has entries:

$$C_{ij} = \sum_{r=1}^k A_{ir} B_{rj} = \text{(row }i\text{ of }A) \cdot \text{(col }j\text{ of }B)$$

Four ways to see matrix multiplication — each gives different geometric insight:

PerspectiveFormulaInsight
Row × Column (standard)$C_{ij} = \mathbf{a}_i^\top \mathbf{b}_j$Each entry is a dot product
Column picture$AB = A[\mathbf{b}_1 \cdots \mathbf{b}_n]$; col $j$ of $C$ = $A$ times col $j$ of $B$$C$'s columns are in col$(A)$
Row pictureRow $i$ of $C$ = row $i$ of $A$ times $B$$C$'s rows are in row$(B)$
Outer product sum$AB = \sum_{r=1}^k \mathbf{a}_{:,r}\,\mathbf{b}_{r,:}$Rank-1 update; used in SVD

Key Properties

PropertyRuleNote
Associativity$(AB)C = A(BC)$Always holds
Distributivity$A(B+C) = AB + AC$Always holds
Non-commutativity$AB \neq BA$ in generalCritical — order matters
Transpose of product$(AB)^\top = B^\top A^\top$Reverses order
Inverse of product$(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$Reverses order (both must be invertible)
Dimension check$A_{m\times k} \cdot B_{k\times n} = C_{m\times n}$Inner dims must match
⚠ Non-Commutativity is the #1 Matrix Trap
In scalar algebra $ab = ba$. In matrix algebra: $AB \neq BA$ in general. Even when both products exist and are the same size, they may differ. However, some pairs do commute: $A$ and $A^{-1}$, $A$ and $I$, $A$ and $f(A)$ for any polynomial $f$.

1.2 Transpose & Inverse

Transpose

$(A^\top)_{ij} = A_{ji}$: reflect across the main diagonal.

IdentityFormula
Double transpose$(A^\top)^\top = A$
Sum$(A+B)^\top = A^\top + B^\top$
Product$(AB)^\top = B^\top A^\top$
Scalar$(cA)^\top = cA^\top$

Symmetric matrix: $A = A^\top$. A symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ always has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors — the spectral theorem. Covariance matrices are always symmetric.

Skew-symmetric: $A = -A^\top$ (all diagonal entries are zero).

Orthogonal matrix: $Q^\top = Q^{-1}$, i.e.\ $Q^\top Q = QQ^\top = I$. The columns (and rows) form an orthonormal basis. Orthogonal matrices preserve lengths and angles: $\|Q\mathbf{x}\| = \|\mathbf{x}\|$.

Matrix Inverse

$A^{-1}$ exists iff $A$ is square and non-singular ($\det A \neq 0$, equivalently $\text{rank}(A) = n$). Then $AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I$.

2×2 Inverse Formula (must memorise)
$$A = \begin{pmatrix}a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix} \implies A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix}d & -b \\ -c & a\end{pmatrix}$$

For larger matrices: use Gaussian elimination (row reduction), cofactor expansion, or the formula $A^{-1} = \text{adj}(A)/\det(A)$.

IdentityFormula
Product inverse$(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$
Transpose inverse$(A^\top)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^\top$
Scalar inverse$(cA)^{-1} = \frac{1}{c}A^{-1}$
Inverse of inverse$(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$
Determinant$\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)$
💡 Sherman-Morrison-Woodbury (Finance Essential)
Updating the inverse of a matrix after a low-rank change is expensive if done naively. The Woodbury identity gives: $$\bigl(A + UCV\bigr)^{-1} = A^{-1} - A^{-1}U\bigl(C^{-1}+VA^{-1}U\bigr)^{-1}VA^{-1}$$ For $U, V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}$, $k \ll n$: reduces cost from $O(n^3)$ to $O(k^2 n)$. Used constantly in portfolio optimisation when adding/removing assets.

1.3 Determinant

The determinant $\det(A)$ is a scalar that encodes the signed volume scaling factor of the linear transformation $A$. If $|\det(A)| > 1$, $A$ expands volume; if $< 1$, it shrinks it; if $= 0$, the transformation collapses space to a lower dimension.

2×2 and 3×3
$$\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$$ $$\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{pmatrix} = a(ei-fh) - b(di-fg) + c(dh-eg)$$

Key Determinant Properties

PropertyRuleProof idea
Product$\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$Volume composition
Transpose$\det(A^\top) = \det(A)$Same volume scaling
Scalar$\det(cA) = c^n \det(A)$ for $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$Each row scaled by $c$
Inverse$\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)$From $\det(AA^{-1})=\det(I)=1$
Triangular$\det(A) = \prod_i A_{ii}$ (product of diagonal)No off-diagonal contribution
Row swapSwapping two rows negates $\det$Sign of permutation
Row scaleMultiplying one row by $c$ scales $\det$ by $c$Multilinearity
Identical rows$\det = 0$ if two rows are identicalLinearly dependent
Eigenvalues$\det(A) = \prod_i \lambda_i$Characteristic polynomial
Invertibility$A$ invertible $\Leftrightarrow \det(A) \neq 0$Core test
📋 Interview: Computing Determinants
Triangular matrices are easy: $\det\begin{pmatrix}3&1&2\\0&4&5\\0&0&2\end{pmatrix} = 3 \times 4 \times 2 = 24$.

The scalar trap: What is $\det(2A)$ for $A \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times 3}$ with $\det(A) = 5$?
Answer: $2^3 \times 5 = 40$, not $2 \times 5 = 10$. Each of 3 rows is scaled by 2.

1.4 Rank, Nullity & the Rank-Nullity Theorem

Definitions

Rank: $\text{rank}(A) = \dim(\text{col}(A)) = \dim(\text{row}(A))$. The number of linearly independent rows equals the number of linearly independent columns (a non-trivial fact).

Nullity: $\text{nullity}(A) = \dim(\text{null}(A)) = $ number of free variables in $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.

Rank-Nullity Theorem (Dimension Theorem)
For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$: $$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n \quad\text{(number of columns)}$$
Intuition: Every column either contributes a pivot (rank) or is a linear combination of previous columns (nullity). Together they exhaust all $n$ columns. Formally: the domain $\mathbb{R}^n$ is partitioned into the row space (dimension = rank, which maps injectively) and the null space (dimension = nullity, which maps to zero).
📋 Example
$A = \begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\4&5&6\\7&8&9\end{pmatrix}$: rows are linearly dependent (row 3 = 2×row 2 − row 1). $\text{rank}(A) = 2$, $\text{nullity}(A) = 3 - 2 = 1$. This means $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has a one-dimensional solution space — verified: $\mathbf{x} = (1,-2,1)^\top$ is in the null space.
ScenarioMeaningConsequence
$\text{rank}(A) = n$ (full column rank)Columns linearly independentNull space = $\{0\}$; $A^\top A$ invertible; unique least-squares solution
$\text{rank}(A) = m$ (full row rank)Rows linearly independent$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ always has a solution
$\text{rank}(A) = n = m$Full rank, square$A$ invertible; unique solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$
$\text{rank}(A) < \min(m,n)$Rank deficientNot invertible; $\det = 0$; infinitely many or no solutions

1.5 Trace

$$\text{tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n A_{ii} = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i$$

Trace equals the sum of eigenvalues. Combined with $\det(A) = \prod_i \lambda_i$, these two facts let you read off the eigenvalues of a 2×2 matrix directly.

Key Trace Properties

PropertyFormula
Linearity$\text{tr}(A+B) = \text{tr}(A)+\text{tr}(B)$, $\text{tr}(cA) = c\,\text{tr}(A)$
Cyclic permutation$\text{tr}(ABC) = \text{tr}(BCA) = \text{tr}(CAB)$ (but not $\text{tr}(ACB)$ in general)
Transpose$\text{tr}(A) = \text{tr}(A^\top)$
Inner product$\text{tr}(A^\top B) = \sum_{i,j} A_{ij}B_{ij}$ — the Frobenius inner product
Frobenius norm$\|A\|_F^2 = \text{tr}(A^\top A) = \sum_{i,j}A_{ij}^2 = \sum_i \sigma_i^2$
Rank of projector$\text{tr}(P) = \text{rank}(P)$ for idempotent $P$
📋 2×2 Eigenvalue Trick via Trace & Determinant
For $A = \begin{pmatrix}4&2\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$: $\text{tr}(A) = 7 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$, $\det(A) = 10 = \lambda_1\lambda_2$.

Eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0 \Rightarrow (\lambda-5)(\lambda-2) = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = 5, 2$.

No need to set up $\det(A - \lambda I)$ from scratch — trace and determinant give you the characteristic polynomial immediately for 2×2 matrices.

Decompositions

2.1 Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

A non-zero vector $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of $A$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$ if:

$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v} \qquad (\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0})$$

Geometric interpretation: $\mathbf{v}$ is a direction that $A$ only stretches (by factor $\lambda$), not rotates. $\lambda < 0$ means it flips direction; $|\lambda| > 1$ means it stretches; $|\lambda| < 1$ means it compresses.

Finding Eigenvalues: Characteristic Equation

$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v} \Rightarrow (A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$. For non-trivial $\mathbf{v}$, the matrix $A - \lambda I$ must be singular:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \qquad \text{(characteristic equation)}$$

This gives a degree-$n$ polynomial in $\lambda$ (the characteristic polynomial), whose $n$ roots (counting multiplicity, possibly complex) are the eigenvalues.

Finding Eigenvectors

For each eigenvalue $\lambda_i$, solve $(A - \lambda_i I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ by row-reducing $A - \lambda_i I$. The solution space is the eigenspace corresponding to $\lambda_i$.

📋 Full Worked Example
$A = \begin{pmatrix}4&2\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$.

Step 1 — Characteristic equation: $\det\begin{pmatrix}4-\lambda & 2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda\end{pmatrix} = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0$

$\Rightarrow \lambda_1 = 5,\;\lambda_2 = 2$

Step 2 — Eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 5$: $(A - 5I)\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}-1&2\\1&-2\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$. Row reduce: $-v_1 + 2v_2 = 0 \Rightarrow v_1 = 2v_2$. Eigenvector: $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$

Step 3 — Eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = 2$: $(A - 2I)\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}2&2\\1&1\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow v_1 = -v_2$. Eigenvector: $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$

Verification: $A\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}4&2\\1&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}10\\5\end{pmatrix} = 5\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$ ✓

2.2 Diagonalisation

An $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalisable if it has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. Then:

$$A = P D P^{-1}$$ where columns of $P$ are eigenvectors, and $D = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$

When is $A$ diagonalisable?

  • $A$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues $\Rightarrow$ always diagonalisable
  • $A$ is symmetric (real) $\Rightarrow$ always diagonalisable (and $P$ is orthogonal, giving $A = Q D Q^\top$, the spectral decomposition)
  • Repeated eigenvalues: need to check if algebraic multiplicity = geometric multiplicity

Power of Diagonalisation

$$A^k = P D^k P^{-1}, \qquad D^k = \text{diag}(\lambda_1^k, \ldots, \lambda_n^k)$$

Computing $A^{100}$ directly requires $O(n^3 \times 100)$ multiplications. With diagonalisation: compute $P$, $D^{100}$ (trivial), $P^{-1}$ — just one $O(n^3)$ factorisation.

📈 Finance: Markov Chain Powers
A transition matrix $P$ for a Markov chain has $P^n$ giving the $n$-step distribution. Diagonalisation makes $P^n = Q D^n Q^{-1}$ tractable. The dominant eigenvalue is always 1 (stationary distribution); if $|\lambda_i| < 1$ for $i > 1$, all non-stationary components decay geometrically.

2.3 Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

SVD is the most important matrix decomposition in applied mathematics. Unlike eigendecomposition, SVD works for any matrix, not just square ones.

Singular Value Decomposition
For any $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with $\text{rank}(A) = r$: $$A = U \Sigma V^\top$$
  • $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$: orthogonal matrix, columns = left singular vectors
  • $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$: diagonal, entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ are the singular values
  • $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$: orthogonal matrix, columns = right singular vectors

Geometric Interpretation

Any linear map $A$ can be decomposed into three steps: (1) $V^\top$ — rotate/reflect the input, (2) $\Sigma$ — stretch along axes by $\sigma_i$, (3) $U$ — rotate/reflect the output. The singular values $\sigma_i$ are the "stretching factors" in orthogonal directions.

Connection to Eigenvalues

$$A^\top A = V \Sigma^\top \Sigma V^\top = V \,\text{diag}(\sigma_1^2, \ldots, \sigma_r^2, 0, \ldots)\, V^\top$$ $$AA^\top = U \Sigma \Sigma^\top U^\top = U \,\text{diag}(\sigma_1^2, \ldots, \sigma_r^2, 0, \ldots)\, U^\top$$

Therefore: singular values of $A$ = $\sqrt{\text{eigenvalues of }A^\top A}$ (= $\sqrt{\text{eigenvalues of }AA^\top}$). The right singular vectors $V$ are eigenvectors of $A^\top A$; the left singular vectors $U$ are eigenvectors of $AA^\top$.

Low-Rank Approximation (Eckart-Young Theorem)

The best rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ in both Frobenius and spectral norm is: $$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i \mathbf{v}_i^\top$$ with approximation error $\|A - A_k\|_F^2 = \sum_{i=k+1}^r \sigma_i^2$

Applications of SVD

ApplicationHow SVD is used
PCASVD of the centred data matrix $X$ gives principal components directly
Rank determination$\text{rank}(A) = $ number of non-zero singular values
Condition number$\kappa(A) = \sigma_1/\sigma_r$ — measures numerical stability; large $\kappa$ = ill-conditioned
Pseudoinverse$A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^\top$ where $\Sigma^+$ replaces each non-zero $\sigma_i$ with $1/\sigma_i$
Image compressionKeep only top-$k$ singular values/vectors — captures most variance
Factor modelsLow-rank approximation of return covariance; systematic vs idiosyncratic risk
📈 SVD in Quantitative Finance
In a factor model, the return covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is decomposed via SVD/eigen-decomposition: $\Sigma \approx \sum_{k=1}^K \sigma_k^2 \mathbf{f}_k\mathbf{f}_k^\top + D$ where $\mathbf{f}_k$ are factor loadings and $D$ is idiosyncratic risk. The first few singular values capture most of the variance — justifying dimension reduction.

2.4 LU Decomposition

LU Decomposition

What: Factor $A = LU$ where $L$ is lower triangular (1s on diagonal) and $U$ is upper triangular.

$$A = LU \qquad \text{or with pivoting: } PA = LU$$

When: $A$ is square. Pivoting ($PA = LU$, $P$ = permutation matrix) is needed for numerical stability when a pivot would be zero or very small.

Why It's Useful

Solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ with LU requires $O(n^3)$ for the factorisation (done once), then two $O(n^2)$ triangular solves:

  1. Factor: $A = LU$ (cost $O(n^3)$)
  2. Forward substitution: solve $L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{b}$ for $\mathbf{y}$ (cost $O(n^2)$)
  3. Back substitution: solve $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$ for $\mathbf{x}$ (cost $O(n^2)$)

For multiple right-hand sides $\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \ldots$, factor once and solve cheaply each time.

⚙ Algorithm (Gaussian Elimination = LU)
LU decomposition is Gaussian elimination in disguise: the elimination multipliers become the entries of $L$, and the row echelon form is $U$. Partial pivoting = swap rows before each elimination step to ensure the largest element is the pivot.

2.5 Cholesky Decomposition

Cholesky Decomposition

Requirement: $A$ must be symmetric positive definite (SPD).

$$A = LL^\top$$ where $L$ is a lower triangular matrix with positive diagonal entries

Why it matters:

  • Twice as efficient as LU for SPD matrices: $O(n^3/3)$ vs $O(2n^3/3)$
  • Numerically stable without pivoting (no cancellation because all diagonal entries are $\sqrt{A_{ii} - \sum \ldots} > 0$ for SPD matrices)
  • Simulating correlated normals: if $\mathbf{Z} \sim N(\mathbf{0}, I)$, then $L\mathbf{Z} \sim N(\mathbf{0}, LL^\top) = N(\mathbf{0}, A)$
📋 Worked Example
$\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix}4&2\\2&3\end{pmatrix}$ (PD: both eigenvalues $> 0$).

$L = \begin{pmatrix}l_{11}&0\\l_{21}&l_{22}\end{pmatrix}$. From $\Sigma = LL^\top$:

$l_{11}^2 = 4 \Rightarrow l_{11} = 2$
$l_{21}l_{11} = 2 \Rightarrow l_{21} = 1$
$l_{21}^2 + l_{22}^2 = 3 \Rightarrow l_{22}^2 = 2 \Rightarrow l_{22} = \sqrt{2}$

$L = \begin{pmatrix}2&0\\1&\sqrt{2}\end{pmatrix}$, verify: $LL^\top = \begin{pmatrix}4&2\\2&3\end{pmatrix}$ ✓
📈 Finance: Simulating Correlated Returns
To simulate $n$ correlated asset returns with covariance $\Sigma$: (1) Cholesky factor $\Sigma = LL^\top$; (2) Generate $\mathbf{Z} \sim N(\mathbf{0}, I_n)$; (3) Transform $\mathbf{R} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + L\mathbf{Z}$. Then $\mathbf{R} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$. This is the standard Monte Carlo approach for multi-asset derivatives.

2.6 QR Decomposition

QR Decomposition

For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with $m \geq n$ and full column rank:

$$A = QR$$
  • $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$: orthonormal columns ($Q^\top Q = I_n$)
  • $R \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$: upper triangular with positive diagonal (unique if required)

Applications:

  • Least squares: $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ solved as $R\mathbf{x} = Q^\top\mathbf{b}$ — numerically superior to normal equations $A^\top A\mathbf{x} = A^\top\mathbf{b}$ (avoids squaring the condition number)
  • Gram-Schmidt: QR decomposition is the matrix formulation of Gram-Schmidt orthogonalisation
  • Eigenvalue algorithms: The QR algorithm iteratively computes eigenvalues of a matrix

Spaces & Geometry

3.1 The Four Fundamental Subspaces

Every matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ gives rise to four fundamental subspaces:

Column Space — $\text{col}(A)$

Span of columns of $A$. Subset of $\mathbb{R}^m$. Dimension = $\text{rank}(A) = r$.

All possible outputs $A\mathbf{x}$. $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ solvable iff $\mathbf{b} \in \text{col}(A)$.

Null Space — $\text{null}(A)$

All $\mathbf{x}$ with $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$. Subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Dimension = nullity = $n - r$.

Orthogonal complement of the row space.

Row Space — $\text{row}(A)$

Span of rows of $A$ = $\text{col}(A^\top)$. Subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Dimension = $r$.

Orthogonal complement of the null space.

Left Null Space — $\text{null}(A^\top)$

All $\mathbf{y}$ with $A^\top\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0}$. Subset of $\mathbb{R}^m$. Dimension = $m - r$.

Orthogonal complement of the column space.

Fundamental Theorem of Linear Algebra (Gilbert Strang)
$$\text{row}(A) \perp \text{null}(A) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{R}^n$$ $$\text{col}(A) \perp \text{null}(A^\top) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{R}^m$$ Every $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ decomposes uniquely as $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_r + \mathbf{x}_n$ where $\mathbf{x}_r \in \text{row}(A)$ and $\mathbf{x}_n \in \text{null}(A)$.

3.2 Orthogonality & Projections

Orthogonality

$\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$ iff $\mathbf{u}^\top\mathbf{v} = 0$. A set $\{\mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_k\}$ is:

  • Orthogonal: $\mathbf{u}_i^\top\mathbf{u}_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$
  • Orthonormal: additionally $\|\mathbf{u}_i\| = 1$, so $\mathbf{u}_i^\top\mathbf{u}_j = \delta_{ij}$

An orthogonal set is automatically linearly independent (can verify: assume $\sum c_i \mathbf{u}_i = 0$, dot both sides with $\mathbf{u}_j$).

Projection onto a Vector

Projection of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$: $$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{a}}\,\mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{a}\mathbf{a}^\top}{\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{a}}\,\mathbf{b}$$ The scalar $\hat{x} = \mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{b}/\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{a}$ is the component of $\mathbf{b}$ along $\mathbf{a}$.

Projection onto a Subspace

Given a subspace spanned by the columns of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ (full column rank), the projection of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\text{col}(A)$ is:

$$\hat{\mathbf{b}} = A(A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top\,\mathbf{b} = P\mathbf{b}$$ where $P = A(A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top$ is the projection matrix (also called hat matrix in regression)

Properties of Projection Matrices

$$P^2 = P \quad\text{(idempotent)} \qquad P^\top = P \quad\text{(symmetric)} \qquad (I-P)^2 = (I-P) \quad\text{(complementary projector)}$$

Eigenvalues of $P$ are only 0 and 1: $\text{rank}(P) = \text{tr}(P) = $ dimension of subspace projected onto.

💡 Least Squares as Projection
When $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ has no solution (overdetermined system), the least-squares solution $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ gives the point in $\text{col}(A)$ closest to $\mathbf{b}$ — it is exactly $\hat{\mathbf{b}} = P\mathbf{b}$. The residual $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}} = (I-P)\mathbf{b}$ is orthogonal to all columns of $A$.

3.3 Gram-Schmidt Process

Given $n$ linearly independent vectors $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n\}$, Gram-Schmidt constructs an orthonormal basis $\{\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_n\}$ for the same subspace:

Gram-Schmidt Algorithm
  1. $\mathbf{u}_1 = \mathbf{v}_1$, $\;\mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{u}_1/\|\mathbf{u}_1\|$
  2. $\mathbf{u}_2 = \mathbf{v}_2 - (\mathbf{v}_2\cdot\mathbf{e}_1)\mathbf{e}_1$, $\;\mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{u}_2/\|\mathbf{u}_2\|$
  3. $\mathbf{u}_3 = \mathbf{v}_3 - (\mathbf{v}_3\cdot\mathbf{e}_1)\mathbf{e}_1 - (\mathbf{v}_3\cdot\mathbf{e}_2)\mathbf{e}_2$, $\;\mathbf{e}_3 = \mathbf{u}_3/\|\mathbf{u}_3\|$
  4. In general: $\mathbf{u}_k = \mathbf{v}_k - \sum_{j=1}^{k-1}(\mathbf{v}_k \cdot \mathbf{e}_j)\,\mathbf{e}_j$, $\;\mathbf{e}_k = \mathbf{u}_k/\|\mathbf{u}_k\|$

At each step, we subtract the component of $\mathbf{v}_k$ along all previously computed directions — the remainder $\mathbf{u}_k$ is orthogonal to all of them.

📋 Worked Example
Orthogonalise $\mathbf{v}_1 = (1,1,0)^\top$ and $\mathbf{v}_2 = (1,0,1)^\top$:

$\mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{v}_1/\|\mathbf{v}_1\| = (1/\sqrt{2},\;1/\sqrt{2},\;0)^\top$

$\mathbf{v}_2 \cdot \mathbf{e}_1 = 1/\sqrt{2}$
$\mathbf{u}_2 = \mathbf{v}_2 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\mathbf{e}_1 = (1,0,1)^\top - \frac{1}{2}(1,1,0)^\top = (1/2,\;-1/2,\;1)^\top$
$\|\mathbf{u}_2\| = \sqrt{1/4+1/4+1} = \sqrt{3/2}$
$\mathbf{e}_2 = (1/\sqrt{6},\;-1/\sqrt{6},\;2/\sqrt{6})^\top$

Verify: $\mathbf{e}_1 \cdot \mathbf{e}_2 = 1/\sqrt{12} - 1/\sqrt{12} + 0 = 0$ ✓

Gram-Schmidt = QR: The matrix with columns $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n$ equals $QR$ where $Q$ has columns $\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_n$ and $R_{jk} = \mathbf{v}_k \cdot \mathbf{e}_j$ (the projection coefficients).

3.4 Change of Basis

A basis $\mathcal{B} = \{\mathbf{b}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n\}$ for $\mathbb{R}^n$ gives an alternative coordinate system. The change-of-basis matrix $P = [\mathbf{b}_1 \cdots \mathbf{b}_n]$ converts $\mathcal{B}$-coordinates to standard coordinates.

$$[\mathbf{x}]_\text{std} = P\,[\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{B} \qquad \Leftrightarrow \qquad [\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{B} = P^{-1}[\mathbf{x}]_\text{std}$$

If $A$ is a linear map expressed in standard basis, its representation in basis $\mathcal{B}$ is:

$$[A]_\mathcal{B} = P^{-1}A\,P$$

This is exactly the diagonalisation formula: if $\mathcal{B}$ is the eigenbasis, $P^{-1}AP = D$ (diagonal).

💡 Why Diagonalisation is Change of Basis
Diagonalisation says: in the eigenvector coordinate system, the linear map $A$ is simply scaling each axis by its eigenvalue. Complicated behaviour in standard coordinates becomes trivial in the right basis. PCA does exactly this: finds the coordinate system (principal components) in which the data's covariance is diagonal.

3.5 Positive Definite Matrices

A symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ is positive definite (PD) if any of these equivalent conditions holds:

Five Equivalent Definitions of Positive Definiteness
  1. $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$ (definition via quadratic form)
  2. All eigenvalues $\lambda_i > 0$
  3. All leading principal minors are positive ($A_{11} > 0$, $\det\begin{pmatrix}A_{11}&A_{12}\\A_{21}&A_{22}\end{pmatrix} > 0$, ...)
  4. Cholesky decomposition $A = LL^\top$ exists with $L$ having positive diagonal
  5. $A = B^\top B$ for some matrix $B$ with full column rank

The PD Hierarchy

TypeSymbolConditionEigenvalues
Positive definite$A \succ 0$$\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} > 0$ for $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$All $> 0$
Positive semi-definite$A \succeq 0$$\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{x}$All $\geq 0$
Negative definite$A \prec 0$$\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} < 0$ for $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$All $< 0$
Indefinite—Both positive and negative valuesMixed signs
📈 PD in Finance
Covariance matrices must be positive semi-definite — they encode variances (non-negative) and correlations. A covariance matrix estimated from data may fail to be PSD if there are more assets than observations ($n > T$). This causes portfolio optimisation to fail. Remedies: shrinkage estimators (Ledoit-Wolf), factor models, or nearest PSD projection.

3.6 Quadratic Forms

A quadratic form in $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is $Q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}$ for a symmetric matrix $A$.

$$Q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n A_{ij}x_i x_j = \sum_i A_{ii}x_i^2 + 2\sum_{i < j}A_{ij}x_i x_j$$

Canonical form via diagonalisation: With $A = Q\Lambda Q^\top$ (spectral theorem), and $\mathbf{y} = Q^\top\mathbf{x}$:

$$\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}^\top \Lambda\mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i y_i^2$$

The quadratic form is a sum of squared terms weighted by eigenvalues. Positive definiteness means all $\lambda_i > 0$, so $Q(\mathbf{x}) > 0$ for $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$.

Quadratic Form Optimisation

On the unit sphere $\|\mathbf{x}\|=1$, the maximum of $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}$ is $\lambda_{\max}$ (achieved at the corresponding eigenvector) and the minimum is $\lambda_{\min}$. The Rayleigh quotient:

$$R(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}}, \quad \lambda_{\min} \leq R(\mathbf{x}) \leq \lambda_{\max}$$
📈 Portfolio Variance as Quadratic Form
Portfolio variance is $\sigma_p^2 = \mathbf{w}^\top \Sigma\mathbf{w}$ where $\mathbf{w}$ is the weight vector and $\Sigma$ the covariance matrix. This is a quadratic form in $\mathbf{w}$. Minimising portfolio variance subject to constraints is a quadratic programming (QP) problem. The minimum variance portfolio corresponds to the smallest eigenvalue direction of $\Sigma$ subject to the budget constraint.

4.1 Systems of Equations $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$

For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ and $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m$, three fundamental cases:

SystemSizeSolutionsCondition
Square, non-singular$m = n$, full rankUnique: $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$$\det(A) \neq 0$
Overdetermined$m > n$Generally no exact solution — use least squares$\mathbf{b} \notin \text{col}(A)$
Underdetermined$m < n$Infinitely many solutions (if consistent)Free variables = nullity
Rank deficientAny, $\text{rank} < \min(m,n)$Either none or infinitely many$\mathbf{b} \in \text{col}(A)$?
Consistency Criterion
$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is consistent (has at least one solution) iff $\mathbf{b} \in \text{col}(A)$, equivalently iff $\text{rank}([A|\mathbf{b}]) = \text{rank}(A)$.

General solution structure: If $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ is any particular solution, then all solutions are:

$$\mathbf{x} = \hat{\mathbf{x}} + \mathbf{x}_n \quad \text{for any } \mathbf{x}_n \in \text{null}(A)$$

The general solution = one particular solution + anything in the null space.

✓ Solving Strategy
  1. Form augmented matrix $[A|\mathbf{b}]$ and row-reduce to RREF
  2. Check consistency: if a row $[0\ 0\ \cdots\ 0\ |\ c]$ with $c \neq 0$ appears, no solution
  3. Identify pivot columns (basic variables) and free columns (free variables)
  4. Express basic variables in terms of free variables; set free variables = parameters

4.2 Least Squares Regression

Given overdetermined $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ ($m > n$, typically no exact solution), find $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ that minimises the sum of squared residuals:

$$\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \arg\min_\mathbf{x} \|\mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}\|^2 = \arg\min_\mathbf{x} \sum_{i=1}^m (b_i - \mathbf{a}_i^\top\mathbf{x})^2$$

Derivation via Normal Equations

The residual $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ must be orthogonal to $\text{col}(A)$ at the optimum (the residual points in the "null of $A^\top$" direction):

Calculus approach: $f(\mathbf{x}) = \|\mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}\|^2 = \mathbf{b}^\top\mathbf{b} - 2\mathbf{b}^\top A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{x}^\top A^\top A\mathbf{x}$.
Setting gradient to zero: $\nabla_\mathbf{x}f = -2A^\top\mathbf{b} + 2A^\top A\mathbf{x} = 0$.
Normal Equations
$$A^\top A\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^\top\mathbf{b}$$ If $A$ has full column rank (columns linearly independent): $$\hat{\mathbf{x}} = (A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top\mathbf{b}$$

Linear Regression Interpretation

For simple linear regression with $n$ data points $(x_i, y_i)$ and model $y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x$:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & x_1 \\ 1 & x_2 \\ \vdots & \vdots \\ 1 & x_n\end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \begin{pmatrix}\hat{\beta}_0\\\hat{\beta}_1\end{pmatrix} = (A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top\mathbf{y}$$ $$\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{Var}(X)}, \quad \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1\bar{x}$$

⚠ Normal Equations vs QR
The formula $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = (A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top\mathbf{b}$ is elegant but numerically fragile: forming $A^\top A$ squares the condition number $\kappa(A^\top A) = \kappa(A)^2$. If $A$ is nearly rank-deficient, $A^\top A$ is very ill-conditioned. In practice, use QR decomposition ($A = QR$, solve $R\hat{\mathbf{x}} = Q^\top\mathbf{b}$) or SVD ($\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^+\mathbf{b}$).
📈 Finance: Factor Model Estimation
In a multi-factor model $\mathbf{r}_t = \alpha + B\mathbf{f}_t + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t$, estimating factor loadings $B$ from time series data is a least squares problem. The columns of $A$ are the factor returns (plus an intercept), and $\mathbf{b}$ is the asset return series. The OLS solution gives the beta exposures. The residuals $\hat{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}$ are the idiosyncratic returns.

4.3 Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

PCA finds the directions of maximum variance in high-dimensional data — a change of basis to the coordinate system in which the data's covariance matrix is diagonal.

PCA Algorithm

  1. Centre the data: Compute $\tilde{X} = X - \bar{X}$ (subtract column means). Data is $n$ observations × $p$ features, $\tilde{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$.
  2. Compute sample covariance: $S = \frac{1}{n-1}\tilde{X}^\top\tilde{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times p}$
  3. Eigendecompose: $S = Q\Lambda Q^\top$ where $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_p \geq 0)$ and columns of $Q$ are eigenvectors (principal components / PCs)
  4. Project: PC scores = $\tilde{X}Q_k$ where $Q_k$ contains the first $k$ eigenvectors (principal components)
  5. Choose $k$: Retain enough PCs to explain a target fraction of total variance (e.g.\ 95%)
Variance Explained
$$\text{Fraction of variance explained by first }k\text{ PCs} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i}{\sum_{i=1}^p \lambda_i} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i}{\text{tr}(S)}$$

PCA via SVD

Compute SVD of the centred data: $\tilde{X} = U\Sigma V^\top$. Then:

  • Columns of $V$ = principal components (same as eigenvectors of $S$)
  • Singular values relate to eigenvalues: $\lambda_i = \sigma_i^2/(n-1)$
  • PC scores = $U\Sigma = \tilde{X}V$

SVD-based PCA is numerically superior to forming $S$ explicitly — avoids the condition number squaring problem.

💡 What PCA Is Actually Doing
PCA solves the sequence of optimisation problems: find direction $\mathbf{w}_1$ maximising $\mathbf{w}_1^\top S\mathbf{w}_1$ subject to $\|\mathbf{w}_1\|=1$. Answer: first eigenvector of $S$. Then find $\mathbf{w}_2$ orthogonal to $\mathbf{w}_1$ maximising variance. Answer: second eigenvector. Etc. PCA is the Rayleigh quotient maximisation chain.
📈 PCA in Fixed Income: Level, Slope, Curvature
Apply PCA to daily changes in yield curve rates (e.g., 2y, 5y, 10y, 30y). Empirically: PC1 ≈ parallel shift of the curve (explains ~80-90% of variance), PC2 ≈ slope change (short vs long rates, ≈ 8-10%), PC3 ≈ curvature (butterfly, ≈ 2-4%). These three PCs explain >95% of yield curve moves. This is the foundation of risk factor decomposition in rates trading.
⚠ PCA Pitfalls
  • Scale sensitivity: PCA is not scale-invariant. If features have vastly different scales, standardise first (divide by standard deviation) — otherwise PCA is dominated by high-variance features regardless of importance.
  • Linearity: PCA finds linear combinations. Non-linear structure (clusters, manifolds) requires kernel PCA or other methods.
  • Interpretability: Principal components are linear combinations of all original features and may be hard to interpret economically.
  • Stationarity: PCA applied to financial returns assumes covariance is stable over time. In practice, correlations shift in crises.

4.4 Covariance Matrices

The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ of a random vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_p)^\top$ is the $p \times p$ matrix:

$$\Sigma_{ij} = \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = E[(X_i - \mu_i)(X_j - \mu_j)]$$ $$\Sigma = E[(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top]$$

Structural Properties

  • Symmetric: $\Sigma = \Sigma^\top$ (since $\text{Cov}(X_i,X_j) = \text{Cov}(X_j,X_i)$)
  • Positive semi-definite: $\mathbf{w}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{w} = \text{Var}(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{X}) \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{w}$
  • Diagonal entries: $\Sigma_{ii} = \text{Var}(X_i) \geq 0$
  • Trace: $\text{tr}(\Sigma) = \sum_i \text{Var}(X_i)$ = total variance
  • Determinant: $\det(\Sigma) = 0$ iff variables are linearly dependent (one is an exact linear combination of others)

Linear Transformation of Covariance

If $\mathbf{Y} = A\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{b}$, then $\text{Cov}(\mathbf{Y}) = A\,\Sigma\,A^\top$

This is the most used formula in quantitative finance. Portfolio variance: $\mathbf{w}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{w}$. Hedged position variance: $(A\mathbf{w})^\top\Sigma(A\mathbf{w})$ where $A$ is the hedge ratio matrix.

Correlation Matrix

$$\text{Corr}(\mathbf{X}) = D^{-1}\Sigma D^{-1} \quad \text{where } D = \text{diag}(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_p)$$ $$\Sigma = D\,\text{Corr}(\mathbf{X})\,D$$

Entries of $\text{Corr}(\mathbf{X})$ are the pairwise correlations $\rho_{ij} \in [-1,1]$, diagonal entries are 1.

📈 High-Dimensional Covariance Estimation
In practice with $p$ assets and $n$ trading days, if $p > n$ the sample covariance is rank deficient (singular) and cannot be inverted for portfolio optimisation. Solutions:
  • Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage: $\hat{\Sigma} = (1-\alpha)S + \alpha T$ where $T$ is a structured target (e.g.\ scaled identity). Pulls extreme sample correlations toward a prior.
  • Factor model: $\Sigma \approx BB^\top + D$ where $B$ are factor loadings (low-rank) and $D$ is diagonal idiosyncratic. Always invertible: $\Sigma^{-1} = D^{-1} - D^{-1}B(I+B^\top D^{-1}B)^{-1}B^\top D^{-1}$ (Woodbury).
  • Random Matrix Theory: Eigenvalues below the Marchenko-Pastur bound are noise — zero them out.
