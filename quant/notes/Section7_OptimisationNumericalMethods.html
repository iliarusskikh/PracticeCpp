Optimisation Theory

Optimisation is the backbone of quantitative finance: calibrating models to market prices, constructing portfolios, training risk models, and pricing American options all reduce to finding a minimum or maximum of some objective function, possibly subject to constraints.

1.1 Convex vs Non-Convex Functions

The single most important structural property of an optimisation problem is convexity. Convex problems guarantee that any local minimum is a global minimum — a property that makes them tractable both theoretically and computationally.

Definition of Convex Function
A function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is convex if, for all $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$ and all $\lambda \in [0,1]$: $$f(\lambda\mathbf{x} + (1-\lambda)\mathbf{y}) \leq \lambda f(\mathbf{x}) + (1-\lambda)f(\mathbf{y})$$ Geometrically: the chord between any two points on the graph lies above (or on) the graph.

Equivalent Conditions for Differentiable Functions

ConditionStatementIntuition
First-order$f(\mathbf{y}) \geq f(\mathbf{x}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{x})^\top(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{x})$ for all $\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}$Graph lies above every tangent hyperplane
Second-orderHessian $H_f(\mathbf{x}) \succeq 0$ (PSD) for all $\mathbf{x}$Curvature is non-negative in all directions
Gradient monotonicity$(\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) - \nabla f(\mathbf{y}))^\top(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}) \geq 0$Gradient is a monotone operator
✓ Convexity Testing Shortcuts
  • Sums: positive-weighted sum of convex functions is convex — $\alpha f + \beta g$ convex when $\alpha,\beta \geq 0$
  • Composition: $g(f(\mathbf{x}))$ is convex if $g$ is convex and non-decreasing and $f$ is convex
  • Max: $\max(f_1,\ldots,f_k)$ is convex whenever each $f_i$ is convex
  • Affine maps: $f(A\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b})$ preserves convexity of $f$
  • Quadratic forms: $\mathbf{x}^\top Q\mathbf{x}$ is convex iff $Q \succeq 0$

Strict Convexity and Strong Convexity

TypeConditionConsequence
Convex$H \succeq 0$Local minimum = global; may not be unique
Strictly convex$H \succ 0$ at all critical pointsAt most one global minimum
Strongly convex (parameter $m$)$H \succeq mI$, $m > 0$ everywhereUnique global min; gradient descent converges at rate $O((1-m/L)^k)$
Non-convex$H$ indefinite somewhereLocal minima may not be global; NP-hard in general
📈 Finance: Why Convexity Matters for Portfolio Optimisation
Mean-variance portfolio optimisation — minimise $\mathbf{w}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{w}$ subject to $\mathbf{1}^\top\mathbf{w}=1$ and $\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{w} \geq \mu^*$ — is a convex quadratic program because $\Sigma \succeq 0$. This guarantees the efficient frontier is a unique globally optimal curve, solvable in polynomial time. Adding cardinality constraints (e.g.\ "at most 20 assets") makes it non-convex and NP-hard.
⚠ Local vs Global Minima — The Non-Convex Trap
Calibrating a Heston stochastic volatility model to implied vol surface is a non-convex optimisation: gradient descent finds a local minimum that may not be globally optimal. Practitioners use multi-start methods (many random initialisations), differential evolution, or basin-hopping to improve global search. Never assume a converged calibration is globally optimal in non-convex settings.

1.2 Unconstrained Optimisation: Gradient Methods

For an unconstrained problem $\min_\mathbf{x} f(\mathbf{x})$, the necessary condition for a local minimum is $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}$ (stationarity). The sufficient second-order condition is $H_f(\mathbf{x}^*) \succ 0$.

Gradient Descent

Gradient Descent Update Rule
$$\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k - \alpha_k \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k)$$ where $\alpha_k > 0$ is the step size (learning rate).

Intuition: move in the direction of steepest descent ($-\nabla f$). Convergence guaranteed for convex $f$ with Lipschitz-continuous gradient $\|\nabla f(\mathbf{x})-\nabla f(\mathbf{y})\| \leq L\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\|$:

$$f(\mathbf{x}_k) - f(\mathbf{x}^*) \leq \frac{L\|\mathbf{x}_0-\mathbf{x}^*\|^2}{2k} \qquad \text{(convergence rate: } O(1/k)\text{)}$$ \text{For strongly convex }f\text{ (parameter }m\text{):} \quad f(\mathbf{x}_k) - f(\mathbf{x}^*) \leq \left(1 - \frac{m}{L}\right)^k [f(\mathbf{x}_0)-f(\mathbf{x}^*)]

Step Size Selection

MethodRuleCost per step
Fixed step size$\alpha_k = \alpha = 1/(2L)$Requires knowing $L$; may diverge if too large
Backtracking line searchStart with $\alpha=1$, halve until Armijo condition holdsA few extra gradient evaluations; robust
Exact line search$\alpha_k = \arg\min_\alpha f(\mathbf{x}_k - \alpha\nabla f)$Inner optimisation; expensive but maximises progress per step

Gradient Ascent

For a maximisation problem $\max_\mathbf{x} f(\mathbf{x})$, simply reverse the sign: $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + \alpha_k \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k)$. In finance, this arises in dual problems, reinforcement learning (policy gradient), and maximum likelihood estimation.

Newton's Method for Optimisation

Replace the gradient descent linear model with a quadratic approximation (use the Hessian):

Newton's Optimisation Update
$$\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k - H_f(\mathbf{x}_k)^{-1}\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k)$$

Newton's method has quadratic convergence near the solution — far superior to gradient descent's linear convergence. The cost is computing and inverting the Hessian at each step: $O(n^3)$ for $n$-dimensional problems. Quasi-Newton methods (BFGS, L-BFGS) approximate the inverse Hessian using gradient differences, achieving superlinear convergence at $O(n^2)$ or $O(n)$ cost respectively.

💡 Convergence Rate Hierarchy — Know This for Interviews
Gradient Descent O(1/k) Linear convergence (convex)
Gradient Descent (SC) $(1-m/L)^k$ Geometric (strongly convex)
Newton / BFGS Quadratic / Superlinear Near solution
Quadratic convergence means: if you have 1 decimal place of accuracy, the next step gives ~2 decimal places, then 4, then 8 — extremely fast near a solution. Newton's method can fail away from the solution (Hessian may not be positive definite).

1.3 KKT Conditions & Constrained Optimisation

The Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions are the fundamental first-order necessary conditions for a solution to a constrained optimisation problem. For differentiable problems, they generalise the Lagrange multiplier conditions to inequality constraints.

Standard Form

Constrained Optimisation Standard Form
$$\min_\mathbf{x} f(\mathbf{x})$$ $$\text{subject to: } g_i(\mathbf{x}) \leq 0, \; i=1,\ldots,m \quad \text{(inequality constraints)}$$ $$h_j(\mathbf{x}) = 0, \; j=1,\ldots,p \quad \text{(equality constraints)}$$

The KKT Conditions

At a local minimum $\mathbf{x}^*$ (assuming regularity / constraint qualification holds):

KKT Necessary Conditions
  1. Stationarity: $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sum_{i=1}^m \mu_i^* \nabla g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sum_{j=1}^p \lambda_j^* \nabla h_j(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}$
  2. Primal feasibility: $g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) \leq 0$ and $h_j(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0$
  3. Dual feasibility: $\mu_i^* \geq 0$ for all $i$ (inequality multipliers non-negative)
  4. Complementary slackness: $\mu_i^* g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0$ for all $i$

$\mu_i^*$ and $\lambda_j^*$ are the KKT multipliers. They have the shadow price interpretation: $\lambda_j^*$ tells you how much the optimal objective value improves per unit relaxation of constraint $j$.

Complementary Slackness — The Key Insight

Condition 4 says: either $\mu_i^* = 0$ (the constraint doesn't actively bind the optimum — "inactive") or $g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0$ (the constraint is exactly at its limit — "active"). Both can be zero together, but they cannot both be strictly positive. This is what makes the KKT conditions a system of equations solvable for the optimal point.

📋 KKT Example: Minimum Variance with Return Target
Minimise $\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{w}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{w}$ subject to $\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{w} \geq \mu^*$ and $\mathbf{1}^\top\mathbf{w} = 1$.

Rewrite inequality as $g(\mathbf{w}) = \mu^* - \boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{w} \leq 0$. Lagrangian:
$\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{w}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{w} + \lambda(1 - \mathbf{1}^\top\mathbf{w}) + \mu(\mu^* - \boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{w})$

Stationarity: $\Sigma\mathbf{w} - \lambda\mathbf{1} - \mu\boldsymbol{\mu} = \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \mathbf{w}^* = \Sigma^{-1}(\lambda\mathbf{1} + \mu\boldsymbol{\mu})$
Complementary slackness: Either $\mu = 0$ (return constraint inactive, minimum variance portfolio regardless of return) or $\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{w} = \mu^*$ (constraint exactly binds at the target).

The efficient frontier traces the optimal $\mathbf{w}^*$ as $\mu^*$ varies from $\min$ to $\max$ expected return.

Sufficiency: When KKT is Also Sufficient

For convex optimisation problems (convex $f$, convex inequality constraint functions $g_i$, affine $h_j$), the KKT conditions are both necessary and sufficient. In this case, solving the KKT system gives the global optimum.

💡 Constraint Qualifications — When KKT Applies
KKT conditions require a constraint qualification (CQ) to hold at the optimum — a regularity condition ensuring the constraints don't degenerate. The most common:
  • LICQ: Active constraint gradients are linearly independent — the gold standard
  • Slater's condition (for convex problems): $\exists\mathbf{x}$ strictly feasible ($g_i(\mathbf{x}) < 0$) — gives strong duality as a bonus
  • MFCQ: There exists a direction decreasing all active constraints
In finance and ML applications, constraints are almost always well-behaved and CQs hold automatically.

1.4 Lagrangian Duality

Duality converts a constrained (primal) problem into an unconstrained (dual) problem. It provides lower bounds on the primal optimal, and for convex problems, the bound is tight — strong duality holds.

The Lagrangian and Dual Function

Lagrangian
$$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\lambda}) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_i \mu_i g_i(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_j \lambda_j h_j(\mathbf{x})$$
Dual Function (always concave — useful!)
$$d(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\lambda}) = \inf_\mathbf{x} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\lambda})$$ $$\text{Dual problem: }\max_{\boldsymbol{\mu} \geq 0, \boldsymbol{\lambda}} d(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\lambda})$$

Weak and Strong Duality

ResultStatementCondition
Weak duality$d(\boldsymbol{\mu},\boldsymbol{\lambda}) \leq p^*$ for all feasible dualsAlways holds — dual is always a lower bound
Strong duality$d^* = p^*$ (duality gap = 0)Convex problem + Slater's condition
Complementary slackness$\mu_i^* g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = 0$At optimality when strong duality holds
📈 Finance: Duality in Risk Measures
The dual of the CVaR (Conditional Value at Risk) minimisation problem has a clean interpretation: the dual variables correspond to probability weights in a "worst-case" distribution. This connects to robust optimisation: optimising the worst-case expected loss over a set of probability measures. The dual problem is often easier to solve or interpret than the primal.

1.5 Linear Programming

A linear program (LP) optimises a linear objective over a polyhedral feasible region defined by linear constraints:

Standard Form LP
$$\min_\mathbf{x} \mathbf{c}^\top\mathbf{x} \quad \text{subject to} \quad A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b},\; \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}$$

Key Properties of LPs

  • Optimal at a vertex: If an LP is bounded and feasible, its optimum is attained at a vertex (extreme point) of the feasible polytope
  • Simplex method: Moves along edges of the polytope from vertex to vertex, always improving the objective — exponential worst case but extremely fast in practice
  • Interior point methods: Polynomial-time algorithms (e.g.\ barrier method); better worst-case guarantees but similar practical speed to simplex
  • LP duality: Every LP has a dual LP; strong duality always holds for feasible bounded LPs (no Slater-type condition needed)
LP Primal–Dual Pair
$$\textbf{Primal: } \min \mathbf{c}^\top\mathbf{x},\; A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b},\; \mathbf{x}\geq0 \qquad \textbf{Dual: } \max \mathbf{b}^\top\mathbf{y},\; A^\top\mathbf{y}\leq\mathbf{c}$$ Strong duality: $\mathbf{c}^\top\mathbf{x}^* = \mathbf{b}^\top\mathbf{y}^*$ (primal opt = dual opt)
📈 Finance Applications of LP
  • No-arbitrage pricing: Detecting arbitrage in a set of asset prices is equivalent to checking LP feasibility
  • Risk budgeting: Allocating risk contributions subject to linear constraints
  • Transaction cost minimisation: Rebalancing a portfolio subject to linear trading constraints
  • CVaR optimisation: Rockafellar-Uryasev (2000) reformulates CVaR minimisation as an LP — making tail risk optimisation tractable

1.6 Quadratic Programming & Portfolio Optimisation

A quadratic program (QP) minimises a quadratic objective subject to linear constraints — the direct formulation of Markowitz mean-variance optimisation:

Standard Form QP
$$\min_\mathbf{x} \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^\top Q\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{c}^\top\mathbf{x} \quad \text{subject to} \quad A\mathbf{x} \leq \mathbf{b},\; C\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{d}$$

When $Q \succeq 0$ (positive semi-definite), this is a convex QP — globally solvable in polynomial time. When $Q \succ 0$, the objective is strictly convex and the solution is unique.

The Markowitz Portfolio QP

$$\min_\mathbf{w} \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{w}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{w} \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{w} = \mu^*,\; \mathbf{1}^\top\mathbf{w} = 1,\; \mathbf{w} \geq \mathbf{0} \text{ (long-only)}$$

The KKT conditions give the closed-form solution when only the two equality constraints are active (no short-selling restriction):

Closed-Form Markowitz Solution (equality constraints only)
$$\mathbf{w}^* = \Sigma^{-1}(\lambda\mathbf{1} + \mu\boldsymbol{\mu}) \qquad \text{with } \lambda, \mu \text{ from solving the } 2\times2 \text{ system:}$$ $$\begin{pmatrix}A & B \\ B & C\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\lambda \\ \mu\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ \mu^*\end{pmatrix}, \quad A=\mathbf{1}^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf{1},\; B=\mathbf{1}^\top\Sigma^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu},\; C=\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\Sigma^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}$$
💡 Two-Fund Separation Theorem
The efficient frontier consists of all convex combinations of just two "mutual funds": the minimum-variance portfolio $\mathbf{w}_\text{mv} = \Sigma^{-1}\mathbf{1}/(\mathbf{1}^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf{1})$ and any other portfolio on the frontier. Every rational mean-variance investor holds some combination of these two funds, regardless of risk preferences. This is the mathematical content of Tobin's two-fund separation theorem.

Numerical Root-Finding & Optimisation

Root-finding — solving $f(x) = 0$ — is ubiquitous in finance: computing bond yields, implied volatility, option prices in implicit finite-difference schemes, and model calibration. Each method makes different trade-offs between speed, robustness, and the information required.

2.1 Bisection Method

Bisection

Idea: Given a bracket $[a, b]$ where $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have opposite signs (by the Intermediate Value Theorem, a root exists), repeatedly halve the interval by evaluating $f$ at the midpoint.

Bisection Algorithm
  1. Require $f(a)\cdot f(b) < 0$ (bracket condition)
  2. Set $m = (a+b)/2$
  3. If $f(m)\cdot f(a) < 0$: set $b=m$; else set $a=m$
  4. Repeat until $|b-a| < \varepsilon$ (tolerance)
Error after $n$ steps: $|x_n - x^*| \leq \frac{b_0 - a_0}{2^n}$
PropertyBisection
Convergence rateLinear — exactly 1 bit of accuracy per step
Steps for $\varepsilon = 10^{-6}$$n \approx 20$ from a unit interval ($2^{-20} \approx 10^{-6}$)
RequirementsContinuity of $f$; a valid bracket
RobustnessGuaranteed to converge — never fails given valid bracket
Derivatives neededNone
📈 Finance: Implied Volatility via Bisection
To find implied vol $\sigma^*$ from a market call price $C_\text{market}$: bracket with $\sigma_\text{lo}=0.001$ (tiny) and $\sigma_\text{hi}=5$ (500%) — the BS call function is strictly increasing in $\sigma$, so $C_\text{BS}(\sigma_\text{lo}) < C_\text{market} < C_\text{BS}(\sigma_\text{hi})$ is easily satisfied. Bisection then finds $\sigma^*$ to 6 decimal places in ~20 iterations. Simple, guaranteed, sufficient for most applications.

2.2 Newton-Raphson Method

Newton-Raphson

Idea: Linearise $f$ at the current iterate using its derivative, and jump to the zero of that linear approximation. Dramatically faster than bisection near the solution.

Newton-Raphson Iteration
$$x_{k+1} = x_k - \frac{f(x_k)}{f'(x_k)}$$

Convergence: Quadratic near a simple root — the number of correct decimal digits roughly doubles per iteration. If $|e_k| = |x_k - x^*|$, then $|e_{k+1}| \approx \frac{|f''(x^*)|}{2|f'(x^*)|}|e_k|^2$.

Derivation: Taylor expand $f$ around $x_k$: $f(x^*) = f(x_k) + f'(x_k)(x^*-x_k) + \frac{1}{2}f''(\xi)(x^*-x_k)^2 = 0$. Rearranging: $x^* = x_k - \frac{f(x_k)}{f'(x_k)} - \frac{f''(\xi)}{2f'(x_k)}(x^*-x_k)^2$. So the Newton iterate $x_{k+1} = x_k - f(x_k)/f'(x_k)$ satisfies $x^* - x_{k+1} = \frac{f''(\xi)}{2f'(x_k)}(x^*-x_k)^2$ — error decreases quadratically.
PropertyNewton-Raphson
Convergence rateQuadratic (near a simple root)
Starting pointMust be "close enough" to the root — can diverge or cycle otherwise
Multiple rootsDegrades to linear convergence at a double root $f(x^*)=f'(x^*)=0$
Requirements$f'$ exists and $f'(x_k) \neq 0$ at each step
Failure modesOscillation, divergence, or convergence to wrong root
📈 Finance: Newton-Raphson for Implied Vol
For implied vol, the Newton step is: $$\sigma_{k+1} = \sigma_k - \frac{C_\text{BS}(\sigma_k) - C_\text{market}}{\mathcal{V}(\sigma_k)}$$ where $\mathcal{V} = \partial C_\text{BS}/\partial\sigma$ is the vega. Since $C_\text{BS}$ is strictly increasing and convex in $\sigma$ with positive vega everywhere, Newton's method converges in 5–10 iterations from a reasonable starting guess. Practitioner Black formula initialises $\sigma_0$ using the ATM approximation $C \approx S\sigma\sqrt{T/2\pi}$, giving $\sigma_0 \approx C\sqrt{2\pi/T}/S$.

Multivariate Newton-Raphson

For a system of equations $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}$ where $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k - J_\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_k)^{-1}\,\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_k)$$

where $J_\mathbf{F}$ is the Jacobian. In practice: solve the linear system $J_\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_k)\,\Delta\mathbf{x} = -\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_k)$ for $\Delta\mathbf{x}$ rather than explicitly inverting the Jacobian. Used in model calibration (calibrating stochastic vol models to multiple strikes simultaneously).

2.3 Secant & Brent's Methods

Secant Method — Newton Without Derivatives

Approximate $f'(x_k)$ by a finite difference using the two most recent iterates:

$$x_{k+1} = x_k - f(x_k)\frac{x_k - x_{k-1}}{f(x_k) - f(x_{k-1})}$$

Convergence rate: superlinear — order $\phi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2 \approx 1.618$ (the golden ratio). Slower than Newton but requires no derivative evaluation.

Brent's Method — The Gold Standard Root-Finder

Brent's method combines bisection, secant, and inverse quadratic interpolation (IQI), choosing the safest step at each iteration:

  • Uses a bracket like bisection — always converges
  • Accelerates with secant/IQI steps when they are safe — nearly as fast as Newton near the root
  • Falls back to bisection when the faster steps would leave the bracket — guaranteed not to fail
✓ When to Use Which Method
  • Bisection: When you need guaranteed convergence and speed doesn't matter; for bracketing
  • Newton-Raphson: When derivatives are cheap, starting point is good, and maximum speed is needed
  • Brent: General-purpose — the right default when you're not sure; guaranteed + fast
  • Secant: When derivatives are unavailable and you have two reasonable starting points

2.4 Gradient Descent Variants

Modern optimisation in machine learning and calibration uses variants of gradient descent designed for large-scale and stochastic settings.

💡 Nesterov Accelerated Gradient — Optimal for Convex Problems
For a convex $L$-smooth function, standard gradient descent achieves $O(1/k)$ convergence. Nesterov (1983) showed that by adding a momentum term: $$\mathbf{y}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k - \frac{1}{L}\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k), \quad \mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{y}_{k+1} + \frac{k-1}{k+2}(\mathbf{y}_{k+1}-\mathbf{y}_k)$$ convergence improves to $O(1/k^2)$ — the optimal rate for first-order methods. This is FISTA (Fast Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm) in the context of sparse optimisation, widely used in regularised regression and lasso.

2.5 Finite Difference Methods for Derivatives

When analytic derivatives are unavailable (or inconvenient), finite differences approximate them numerically. This is essential for computing Greeks when no closed-form derivative exists.

Three Standard Finite Difference Approximations
$$f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \quad \text{(forward difference, } O(h)\text{ error)}$$ $$f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x)-f(x-h)}{h} \quad \text{(backward difference, } O(h)\text{ error)}$$ $$f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h)-f(x-h)}{2h} \quad \text{(central difference, } O(h^2)\text{ error)}$$
Second Derivative
$$f''(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - 2f(x) + f(x-h)}{h^2} \quad (O(h^2)\text{ error})$$

The Step Size Trade-off

Choosing $h$ involves a fundamental trade-off:

  • Too large $h$: truncation error dominates (approximation error from the Taylor expansion is large)
  • Too small $h$: cancellation error dominates (subtracting nearly equal floating-point numbers loses precision)
$$h_\text{opt,forward} \approx \sqrt{\varepsilon_\text{machine}} \approx 10^{-8} \qquad h_\text{opt,central} \approx \varepsilon_\text{machine}^{1/3} \approx 10^{-5}$$

Central differences are strongly preferred — they achieve $O(h^2)$ accuracy vs $O(h)$ for forward differences, at the cost of one extra function evaluation. For computing Greeks in Monte Carlo, the central difference with $h = 0.01S$ is standard.

📈 Finance: Numerical Greeks
For exotic options with no closed-form Greeks, finite differences on the pricing function directly: $$\Delta \approx \frac{V(S+h) - V(S-h)}{2h}, \qquad \Gamma \approx \frac{V(S+h)-2V(S)+V(S-h)}{h^2}$$ Practical choices: $h = 0.01S$ for delta/gamma in equity options. For vega: bump $\sigma$ by 1 bp. For theta: reprice with $T \to T - 1/252$. These "bumping" calculations are standard in risk systems.

ODE & PDE Solvers

Stochastic differential equations (SDEs) are the primary modelling tool for asset prices, interest rates, and volatility. When closed-form solutions don't exist, we simulate paths numerically using ODE/SDE discretisation schemes.

3.1 Euler Methods

For an ODE $\frac{dy}{dt} = f(y, t)$ with initial condition $y(0) = y_0$, discretise time into steps of size $\Delta t$.

Explicit (Forward) Euler Method

$$y_{n+1} = y_n + \Delta t\, f(y_n, t_n)$$

Local truncation error: $O(\Delta t^2)$. Global error: $O(\Delta t)$ — "first-order accurate."

Strengths: Simplest possible scheme; explicit — no system of equations to solve at each step.

Weaknesses: Conditionally stable — requires $\Delta t < 2/|\partial f/\partial y|$ for stability. Can become unstable for stiff equations.

Implicit (Backward) Euler Method

$$y_{n+1} = y_n + \Delta t\, f(y_{n+1}, t_{n+1})$$

Requires solving a (possibly nonlinear) equation for $y_{n+1}$ at each step — but is unconditionally stable. Essential for stiff equations (e.g.\ the PDE arising from barrier options with a fine grid near the barrier).

Euler-Maruyama: The SDE Extension

For an SDE $dX_t = \mu(X_t,t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t,t)\,dW_t$, the Euler-Maruyama scheme is the direct Euler extension:

Euler-Maruyama Scheme
$$X_{n+1} = X_n + \mu(X_n, t_n)\Delta t + \sigma(X_n, t_n)\,\Delta W_n, \quad \Delta W_n \sim N(0, \Delta t)$$

Strong order of convergence: $O(\sqrt{\Delta t})$. Weak order (for expectations): $O(\Delta t)$. For GBM $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$, Euler-Maruyama can cause $S$ to go negative for large $\Delta t$ — the log-Euler (Milstein on the log) or exact simulation should be used instead.

⚠ Euler-Maruyama on GBM — Use the Log Scheme
Applying Euler-Maruyama directly to $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$ gives $S_{n+1} = S_n(1 + \mu\Delta t + \sigma\Delta W_n)$, which can become negative for large $\sigma$ or $\Delta t$. The correct approach is to simulate $\ln S$ exactly: $\ln S_{t+\Delta t} = \ln S_t + (\mu - \sigma^2/2)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,Z$ where $Z \sim N(0,1)$. This gives the exact GBM distribution at each step regardless of $\Delta t$.

3.2 Runge-Kutta Methods

Runge-Kutta methods achieve higher-order accuracy by evaluating $f$ at intermediate points within each time step, then taking a weighted average. No extra memory is required beyond the current state.

RK4 — The Classic Fourth-Order Method

RK4 — Fourth-Order Runge-Kutta
$$k_1 = f(y_n, t_n)$$ $$k_2 = f\!\left(y_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}k_1,\; t_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\right)$$ $$k_3 = f\!\left(y_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}k_2,\; t_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\right)$$ $$k_4 = f\!\left(y_n + \Delta t\, k_3,\; t_n + \Delta t\right)$$ $$y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{\Delta t}{6}\left(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4\right)$$

Local error: $O(\Delta t^5)$. Global error: $O(\Delta t^4)$. Requires 4 function evaluations per step.

📋 Milstein Scheme — One Order Better Than Euler-Maruyama
For $dX = \mu(X)\,dt + \sigma(X)\,dW$, the Milstein correction adds a term capturing the Itô correction more accurately: $$X_{n+1} = X_n + \mu\Delta t + \sigma\Delta W_n + \frac{1}{2}\sigma\sigma'\left[(\Delta W_n)^2 - \Delta t\right]$$ For GBM ($\sigma(X) = \sigma X$, $\sigma' = \sigma$): the Milstein scheme with $\ln S$ simulation is exact. The extra term converts strong convergence from $O(\sqrt{\Delta t})$ to $O(\Delta t)$ — a meaningful improvement for path-dependent option pricing.

3.3 Finite Differences for PDEs

The finite difference method (FDM) discretises a PDE on a grid in $(S, t)$-space, converting the PDE into a system of algebraic equations. The three canonical schemes differ in how they treat the time derivative.

Grid Setup for Option Pricing PDE
Time: $t_0 = 0, t_1, \ldots, t_M = T$ with step $\Delta t = T/M$
Asset: $S_0 = 0, S_1, \ldots, S_N = S_\text{max}$ with step $\Delta S$
Unknowns: $V_j^n \approx V(S_j, t_n)$

3.4 Black-Scholes via Finite Difference

The BS PDE is a standard parabolic PDE, solved backwards from the terminal condition (payoff at expiry).

Discretised BS PDE (Crank-Nicolson in practice)
$$\frac{V_j^{n+1} - V_j^n}{\Delta t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S_j^2 \frac{V_{j+1}^n - 2V_j^n + V_{j-1}^n}{\Delta S^2} + rS_j \frac{V_{j+1}^n - V_{j-1}^n}{2\Delta S} - rV_j^n = 0$$

Boundary conditions:

  • At $S=0$: $V(0,t) = 0$ for a call (stock worthless → option worthless)
  • At $S=S_\text{max}$: $V(S_\text{max},t) \approx S_\text{max} - Ke^{-r(T-t)}$ (deep ITM call ≈ stock minus discounted strike)
  • At $t=T$: $V_j^M = \max(S_j - K, 0)$ (call payoff)
💡 Why FDM Beats Monte Carlo for American Options
American options require checking the early-exercise condition at every point in $(S, t)$ space. In the FDM, this is natural: at each grid node, the American value is $V_j^n = \max(V_j^n|_\text{continuation},\; \text{intrinsic})$. In Monte Carlo, computing the optimal stopping surface requires regression-based methods (Longstaff-Schwartz). FDM is therefore the industry standard for American equity options; Monte Carlo is preferred for high-dimensional or path-dependent payoffs.
4
Monte Carlo Methods

Monte Carlo (MC) is the workhorse of quantitative finance for high-dimensional or path-dependent pricing problems. At its core: approximate $E[g(X)] \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N g(X^{(i)})$ by simulating $N$ independent samples $X^{(i)}$. The central challenge is achieving sufficient accuracy at manageable computational cost.

4.1 Simulating from Distributions

Inverse CDF (Probability Integral Transform) Method

The fundamental simulation technique: if $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and $F$ is a CDF, then $X = F^{-1}(U)$ has distribution $F$.

Inverse CDF Sampling
Given $U \sim U(0,1)$, set $X = F^{-1}(U)$. Then $X \sim F$.
Proof: $P(X \leq x) = P(F^{-1}(U) \leq x) = P(U \leq F(x)) = F(x)$ ✓
DistributionInverse CDF / Simulation Method
Uniform$(a,b)$$X = a + (b-a)U$
Exponential$(\lambda)$$X = -\ln(U)/\lambda$ (exact inverse CDF)
Geometric$(p)$$X = \lceil \ln(U)/\ln(1-p) \rceil$
Normal$N(0,1)$Box-Muller or inverse CDF (no closed form; use numerical $\Phi^{-1}$)
Log-Normal$X = e^{\mu + \sigma Z}$, $Z \sim N(0,1)$
Poisson$(\lambda)$Inverse CDF on PMF; or: generate exponentials until sum exceeds 1

Box-Muller Transform — Generating Standard Normals

Generate two independent $U(0,1)$ variates and transform to two independent normals:

Box-Muller
Let $U_1, U_2 \sim U(0,1)$ independent. Then: $$Z_1 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1}\cos(2\pi U_2), \qquad Z_2 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1}\sin(2\pi U_2)$$ are independent standard normal random variables.

Simulating Correlated Normals

To generate an $n$-dimensional normal vector $\mathbf{X} \sim N(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$:

  1. Cholesky factor: $\Sigma = LL^\top$
  2. Generate $\mathbf{Z} = (Z_1, \ldots, Z_n)^\top$ with i.i.d. $Z_i \sim N(0,1)$
  3. Transform: $\mathbf{X} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + L\mathbf{Z}$

Verification: $\text{Cov}(\mathbf{X}) = L\,\text{Cov}(\mathbf{Z})\,L^\top = L\,I\,L^\top = \Sigma$ ✓. Used for multi-asset Monte Carlo, Cholesky-correlated Brownian paths, and copula-based credit models.

Acceptance-Rejection Sampling

For distributions where the inverse CDF has no closed form, acceptance-rejection samples from a proposal distribution $g(x)$ that envelopes the target $f(x) \leq cg(x)$:

  1. Draw $X \sim g$ and $U \sim U(0,1)$
  2. Accept $X$ if $U \leq f(X)/(cg(X))$; otherwise reject and repeat
  3. Accepted samples are distributed according to $f$

Acceptance probability is $1/c$ — efficiency depends on how tightly $g$ envelopes $f$. For the standard normal, the optimal proposal achieves $c = \sqrt{2e/\pi} \approx 1.32$ (about 76% acceptance).

4.2 Antithetic Variates

Antithetic variates exploit the symmetry of the normal distribution to pair each simulation with its "mirror image," creating negative correlation between pairs of payoffs and reducing variance.

Antithetic Variates Estimator
For each $Z^{(i)} \sim N(0,1)$, use both $Z^{(i)}$ and $-Z^{(i)}$ to generate two paths: $$S_T^+ = S_0\exp\!\left((r-\tfrac{\sigma^2}{2})T + \sigma\sqrt{T}\,Z^{(i)}\right), \quad S_T^- = S_0\exp\!\left((r-\tfrac{\sigma^2}{2})T - \sigma\sqrt{T}\,Z^{(i)}\right)$$ Estimate: $\hat{V} = e^{-rT}\cdot\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N/2}\frac{g(S_T^+)+g(S_T^-)}{2}$
Variance Reduction Formula
$$\text{Var}\!\left(\frac{g^+ + g^-}{2}\right) = \frac{\text{Var}(g)}{2} + \frac{\text{Cov}(g^+, g^-)}{2}$$ Variance reduction occurs whenever $\text{Cov}(g^+, g^-) < 0$ — guaranteed for monotone payoff functions $g$ (call payoffs are monotone increasing in $S_T$, so $S_T^+ > S_T^-$ implies $g^+ > g^-$, giving negative covariance).
Why antithetic works for calls: Since $Z$ and $-Z$ have the same distribution (symmetry of the normal), $g^+ = \max(S_T^+-K,0)$ and $g^- = \max(S_T^--K,0)$ are identically distributed but negatively correlated: when $Z$ is large (high $S^+$, so $g^+$ is large), $-Z$ is large negative (low $S^-$, so $g^-$ is likely small). This negative correlation reduces the variance of their average. Effective VR factor: typically 2–5× for standard call options.
⚠ Antithetic Variates for Path-Dependent Options
For options whose payoff depends on the entire path (e.g.\ arithmetic Asian options), both paths $\{S^+\}$ and $\{S^-\}$ must be fully simulated using the antithetic $-Z$ at each time step. The negative correlation carries through as long as the path functional is monotone in the driving Brownian motion. Non-monotone payoffs (butterfly spreads, some barrier options) may actually increase variance — always test empirically.

4.3 Control Variates

A control variate is a random variable $Y$ correlated with the payoff $g(X)$ whose expectation $E[Y] = \mu_Y$ is known analytically. By exploiting the correlation, we reduce the variance of the estimator.

Control Variate Estimator
$$\hat{V}_c = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N\left[g(X^{(i)}) - c(Y^{(i)} - \mu_Y)\right]$$ This estimator has expectation $E[g(X)]$ for any value of $c$ — the control does not introduce bias. $$\text{Var}(\hat{V}_c) = \text{Var}(g) + c^2\,\text{Var}(Y) - 2c\,\text{Cov}(g, Y)$$
Optimal Control Coefficient
Minimise over $c$: $\quad c^* = \frac{\text{Cov}(g, Y)}{\text{Var}(Y)} = \rho_{gY}\frac{\text{SD}(g)}{\text{SD}(Y)}$
Variance reduction factor at optimal $c^*$: $$\frac{\text{Var}(\hat{V}_{c^*})}{\text{Var}(\hat{V}_0)} = 1 - \rho_{gY}^2$$ A control with $|\rho| = 0.95$ reduces variance by $1 - 0.9025 = 9.75\%$... wait, it reduces to $1-0.9025 = 0.0975$ of original — a $\mathbf{10\times}$ speedup.
📋 Classic Control Variates in Finance
(a) Asian option pricing: Arithmetic Asian price (unknown closed form) correlated with geometric Asian price (closed form exists). Typical $|\rho| \approx 0.98$, giving ~25× variance reduction.

(b) Basket option: Use a product of the individual Black-Scholes prices as a control (their expectation is known). The basket payoff and the control are highly correlated through the shared Brownian drivers.

(c) European option with path simulation: When pricing a path-dependent option, use the terminal stock price's contribution to a vanilla option as a control — since $E[e^{-rT}\max(S_T-K,0)]$ is given by Black-Scholes.

Practical note: Estimate $c^*$ from a pilot run of 1000 simulations (estimate $\hat{c}^* = \hat{\text{Cov}}/\hat{\text{Var}}$), then use it in the main run. The bias from estimating $c^*$ is $O(1/N)$ — negligible for $N \geq 10000$.

4.4 Importance Sampling

Importance sampling (IS) is the most powerful variance reduction technique when dealing with rare events — deep out-of-the-money options, credit portfolio losses, or tail risk measures.

The Core Identity

Importance Sampling Identity
$$E_f[g(X)] = \int g(x)\,f(x)\,dx = \int g(x)\,\frac{f(x)}{q(x)}\,q(x)\,dx = E_q\!\left[g(X)\frac{f(X)}{q(X)}\right]$$ where $q$ is the proposal distribution and $w(X) = f(X)/q(X)$ is the likelihood ratio (importance weight).
IS Estimator
$$\hat{V}_\text{IS} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N g(X^{(i)})\,w(X^{(i)}), \quad X^{(i)} \sim q$$ The IS estimator is unbiased for any valid proposal $q$ (with $q(x)>0$ wherever $f(x)g(x)>0$).

Optimal Proposal Distribution

The optimal $q^*$ that minimises the IS variance is:

$$q^*(x) = \frac{|g(x)|\,f(x)}{E_f[|g(X)|]}$$ With this proposal, all samples contribute equally — zero variance! Of course, $E_f[|g(X)|]$ is what we're trying to estimate, so $q^*$ is not directly computable. But this points the way: sample more from regions where $|g(x)f(x)|$ is large.

Exponential Tilting — IS for Normal Distributions

For payoffs driven by normal $Z \sim N(0,1)$, shift the mean to put more probability mass in the relevant region:

Exponential Tilting (Normal Shift)
Under shifted measure $Z' = Z - \theta \sim N(0,1)$ where $Z = Z' + \theta$:
Original density: $f(Z) = \phi(Z)$. Proposal density: $q(Z) = \phi(Z-\theta)$.
Likelihood ratio: $w = \frac{f(Z)}{q(Z)} = \frac{\phi(Z)}{\phi(Z-\theta)} = e^{-\theta Z + \theta^2/2}$

For a deep OTM call: choose $\theta$ so that $E_q[S_T] \approx K$ — centre the simulation at the strike.
📋 Importance Sampling for Deep OTM Options (Verified)
Call option: $S=100$, $K=150$ (50% OTM), $\sigma=20\%$, $T=1$yr, $r=0$.
BS exact price: $0.0038$ (very small — deep OTM).

Plain MC (100,000 paths): only ~0.1% of paths expire ITM. Price estimate has huge relative error (SE/price $\approx 30\%$).

IS with $\theta = \ln(150/100)/0.20 = 2.03$ (shift to put mean at strike): ~50% of shifted paths expire ITM. Price estimate has SE/price $< 1\%$. Variance reduction factor: $\approx 900\times$.

This is why IS is the standard tool for CVaR computation and pricing tail options.

4.5 Quasi-Monte Carlo

Standard MC uses pseudo-random numbers — they appear random but are systematically generated. Quasi-Monte Carlo (QMC) replaces them with low-discrepancy sequences that cover the unit hypercube more uniformly than pseudo-random numbers, dramatically improving convergence.

Discrepancy and the Koksma-Hlawka Bound

Convergence Comparison
$$\text{Standard MC: } \text{error} = O\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \qquad \text{QMC: } \text{error} = O\!\left(\frac{(\log N)^d}{N}\right)$$ where $d$ is the dimension. For low-to-moderate $d$, QMC achieves effective $O(1/N)$ convergence — equivalent to infinite-order MC.

Common Low-Discrepancy Sequences

SobolBase-2 digital construction with direction numbersHigh dimensions ($d$ up to several hundred); industry standard
FaurePermuted Halton in same prime baseModerate dimensions with scrambling
Lattice rulesEvenly spaced on a lattice in $[0,1]^d$Smooth integrands with known smoothness
💡 Brownian Bridge Construction for QMC Path Simulation
Naively applying a $d$-dimensional Sobol sequence to simulate a path of $d$ time steps wastes its uniformity — the first dimension is most important (determines endpoint; contributes most to option payoff variance) but in standard time-stepping, later dimensions are just refinements. The Brownian bridge construction reorders: first simulate the endpoint $W_T$ (most important), then the midpoint $W_{T/2}$, then the quarter points, and so on. This aligns the most uniform QMC dimensions with the most important integration directions, greatly improving QMC performance for path-dependent options.
⚠ QMC Pitfalls — When It Doesn't Help
  • Very high dimension: The $({\log N})^d/N$ bound degrades as $d$ grows. QMC loses its advantage over MC for $d \geq 300$–$500$ without dimension reduction.
  • Discontinuous payoffs: QMC convergence proofs assume smooth integrands. Discontinuous payoffs (digital options, barrier options) require smoothing (e.g.\ smoothing the indicator function) to benefit from QMC.
  • Standard errors: Unlike MC, QMC doesn't have a simple standard error formula — use randomised QMC (add a random shift to the sequence) to estimate error via multiple independent randomisations.

4.6 Stratified Sampling & Other Techniques

Stratified Sampling

Divide the sample space into $K$ strata, allocate $n_k$ samples to each stratum, and sample uniformly within each. The estimator is:

$$\hat{V}_\text{strat} = \sum_{k=1}^K p_k\,\bar{g}_k, \quad \bar{g}_k = \frac{1}{n_k}\sum_{j=1}^{n_k}g(X_j^{(k)})$$

where $p_k$ is the probability mass of stratum $k$. Optimal allocation (proportional to $\sigma_k$): $n_k \propto p_k\sigma_k$ (Neyman allocation). Variance reduction factor: at least $K\times$ for equal strata with uniform allocation.

Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS)

A generalisation of stratified sampling to $d$ dimensions. Divide each of $d$ dimensions into $N$ equal intervals; sample exactly one point from each interval in each dimension, with random pairing across dimensions. Guarantees perfect stratification in every margin while being easy to implement:

$$U_j^{(i)} = \frac{\pi_j(i) - U_{ij}}{N}, \quad i=1,\ldots,N;\; j=1,\ldots,d$$

where $\pi_j$ is a random permutation of $\{1,\ldots,N\}$ and $U_{ij}\sim U(0,1)$. LHS achieves better coverage than i.i.d. sampling with no extra cost — a drop-in replacement for the pseudo-random number generator in any MC engine.

Moment Matching

Adjust simulated samples to exactly match known moments of the target distribution. For normal samples: standardise to mean 0, variance 1, and optionally adjust skewness/kurtosis. Useful when the option payoff is very sensitive to the mean (forward price):

Adjust samples $Z_1,\ldots,Z_N$ to have $\bar{Z}=0$ and $\hat{\sigma}^2=1$ exactly: $\tilde{Z}_i = (Z_i - \bar{Z})/\hat{\sigma}$. This eliminates the simulation error in moments and is free — apply before every MC run.

💡 The Fundamental MC Trade-off
All variance reduction techniques share the same goal: reduce the variance of the estimator without introducing bias, so that fewer paths are needed to achieve a target accuracy. The standard error of plain MC is $\sigma/\sqrt{N}$ — to reduce error by 10×, you need 100× more paths. A 100× variance reduction achieves the same accuracy improvement at no extra computational cost. In production systems, combining IS + QMC + antithetic + control variates routinely achieves 1000×–10000× effective variance reduction, making exotic option pricing tractable.
